## What Was Explored

Simon brought the full bottom-up distribution problem: Motion D needs to be active (not just a passive blog funnel), the "agent marketplace" framing feels dead, the lovable analogy is interesting but imprecise, and the question of where to find demand for the "build an agent" capability has no obvious answer. We also explored whether Claude Cowork / ChatGPT / Perplexity power users represent a new distribution surface. Another model's advice on decomposing "builder strategy" into four separate tests (supply, distribution, usage, revenue) was brought in and pressure-tested. The session produced a concrete execution playbook with six experiments, kill criteria, and an 8-week timeline.

## What Emerged

- **"Bottom-up builder strategy" is at least four separate tests.** Supply (will someone package a workflow?), distribution (will they share it?), usage (will operators return?), revenue (will anyone pay for tooling?). Each experiment should be tagged to which test it serves. A Product Hunt launch tests attention, not supply. Knowing which question you're answering before you run the test is the difference between learning and flailing. (Credit to external model for this decomposition; validated and adopted.)

- **Power-user upgrade path is the fastest-signal distribution angle.** Cowork/ChatGPT/Perplexity users are already building agent workflows and losing context every session. All three platforms have weak or nonexistent cross-session memory, zero portability. The pitch — "your agent forgets everything every session, fix that in 5 minutes" — requires zero category education. The pain is public (Twitter/Reddit frustration threads). This angle tests usage directly, not supply. Missing from the other model's analysis, which was entirely supply-side.

- **The lovable analogy is right about the experience, wrong about the demand pre-existence.** Lovable works because "I want an app" is a desire millions already have. "I want an agent" barely exists yet as buyer-language. The implication: you can't just build the lovable experience and wait for demand — you need to meet people where the demand already exists (workflow sellers, power users, consultants) and upgrade them into the mounted-agent model.

- **Agent marketplace framing is dead as a positioning play.** Every agent marketplace (GPT Store, ClawMart, Agent.ai, Kamoto.AI) has failed or stalled. Root cause: the agents are commodities (static prompts, no memory, no tools). A marketplace of commodities collapses. Tokenrip's agents are structurally different but the word "marketplace" pattern-matches to the dead ones. Kill the framing. Marketplace emerges from density as Motion C (2027-2028) — it's the destination, not the pitch.

- **Static workflow sellers are the most immediately reachable builder population.** People already selling prompt packs, AI consulting methodologies, Notion templates, ClawMart agents — they've solved supply and distribution already. The question is whether the mounted-agent upgrade is compelling enough. This is more tractable than finding "agent builders" because these people have something concrete to package today.

- **Bridge terms matter — "mounted agent" is architecture language, not buyer language.** The market needs a bridge term that normal people understand. "Workflow agent" attracts automation people (wrong). "Expertise agent" is closer but niche. Working hypothesis: use different terms for different experiments and let response data reveal the winner. Don't lock prematurely.

- **$500-$2K paid build offers are Motion A in disguise.** The other model suggested offering paid agent-building as a service. This is consulting revenue dressed up as platform validation. The cleaner test of revenue willingness is whether anyone upgrades to a paid tooling tier — that tests platform pull, not services appetite.

- **Motion A deprecation rationale revisited for angel context.** Three reasons: (1) fails the architectural-requirement test (any SaaS could ship a $25K vertical pilot), (2) poisons Motion B (firm pilots look like competitive wedge-and-replace to vendors you'd want as substrate licensees), (3) produces flat-dot revenue that doesn't compound into a fundable trajectory. The angel's instinct (prove willingness-to-pay) is valid but answered by tooling-tier conversion, not by consulting-style pilots.

- **Two-month bounded experiment window is structurally sound.** Accelerator decisions land within this window. Distribution testing happens regardless of outcome. Kill criteria per experiment prevent diffuse effort. If nothing works in 8 weeks, the data on what didn't work informs the next move (including potentially revisiting Motion A with eyes open).
