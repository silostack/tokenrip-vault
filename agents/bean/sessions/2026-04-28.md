# Session — 2026-04-28

**Ideas touched**: [[mounted-agent-model]], [[chief-of-staff-agent]]

## What Was Explored

Simon brought the naming question for the new agent category. Internal "cloud agents" was acknowledged as generic and partially taken (Salesforce Cloud Agents, Lyzr-style headless agents); session worked through architectural-pattern alternatives, the noun for the canonical artifact, and the linguistic form (`-ed` vs `-able`). Landed on **mounted agents** as the category and **imprint** as the noun for the thing that gets mounted.

## What Emerged

- **The inversion is the architectural truth, not "cloud."** "Cloud agent" was describing location (somewhere else) when the distinctive thing is *decomposition* (brain on substrate, runtime is BYO). Industry has named this pattern before — headless CMS, self-hosted, BYO-anything. The naming should make the inversion visible, not hide it under a generic label.
- **"Headless" partially fits but Lyzr already owns the narrower definition.** Lyzr-style "headless agent" = no UI, accessed via API. Tokenrip's mounted agent = no fixed UI + no bundled brain + no bundled runtime. Claiming "headless 2.0" was tempting but the prior usage is shallow-but-not-ignorable; the cost of redefining outweighs the inheritance benefit.
- **Architectural-pattern names beat metaphor names for category formation.** Containers, serverless, microservices, headless CMS, edge computing, JAMstack, cloud-native — every winning category name in infra is a boring technical pattern description, not a cute metaphor. Cute metaphors stay at product/nickname tier (Lambda, Pods); they rarely rise to category names. The test: would competitors voluntarily adopt the term? "Mounted agents" passes. "Cartridge agents" doesn't — sounds like an insult coming from a competitor.
- **The canonical noun creates a vocabulary tree.** Whatever you call the central artifact (image, manifest, soul, imprint) determines what its registry, fork action, versioning system, and "base X" all sound like. Soul-vocabulary breaks in 50% of natural use ("soul registry," "soul versioning," "base soul" all feel wrong). Imprint-vocabulary holds across all use cases due to its publishing heritage. The cost of picking the wrong noun isn't immediate — it accumulates every time you write technical docs or have an engineering conversation.
- **Imprint > soul because external/propagatable beats internal/sacred for portable architecture.** Soul implies uniqueness, sacredness, singular essence. Imprint implies replication, distribution, propagation — exactly what a brain-on-a-substrate-mounted-into-many-harnesses needs to be. You can fork an imprint comfortably. Forking a soul is theologically weird. The architecture is fundamentally about the duplicable, not the singular — pick the noun that fits.
- **Adopting a competitor-coined term confirms their category.** OpenClaw popularized "soul" for agent identity. Adopting it would put Tokenrip on OpenClaw's shelf as a follower, even where the term partially fits. Yesterday's "naming determines shelf placement" insight extends one level deeper — to terminology adoption, not just category labels. Worth paying education cost for differentiation when the competitor's framing doesn't fully fit your architecture.
- **Past-participle (`-ed`) beats capability (`-able`) for tech category names.** Containerized, hosted, managed, distributed — all describe architectural state, not abstract capability. The convention reads `-ed` as "built for X-based deployment," not "currently in X-state." "Mounted agents" inherits this convention cleanly; "mountable agents" would be linguistically more precise but category-defining-weaker. Tech-category precedent is overwhelming on this — don't fight it.
