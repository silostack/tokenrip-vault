## What Was Explored

Simon brought the ICP landscape behind the bottom-up distribution playbook (produced yesterday). Four threads: OpenClaw/Hermes users as collaboration-oriented rather than agent-building; AI agencies as potential infrastructure buyers; the "new normal worker" emerging in the workforce (grounded in a real text conversation with a friend who went from zero code to VS Code automations in three months); and a YC investor who published an office hours skill and asked for a demo. The session produced a full ICP taxonomy with six distinct buyer populations and a new experiment (Published-but-Blind upgrade).

## What Emerged

- **Three new ICPs identified that weren't in the playbook.** (1) "Published but Blind" — people who shipped an agent/skill/GPT into the world and have no feedback mechanism. They've solved supply and distribution; they're stuck on visibility. Distinct from workflow sellers (who haven't shipped an agent) and vertical builders (who are building products). (2) "AI Enabler" — the emerging internal company role that builds/deploys agents for coworkers. The builder in the builder/operator split for enterprise. Bridges Motion D → B. (3) "New Normal Worker" — non-technical workers like Glo who are automating their jobs with AI and will mount agents others build. Demand side, not supply.

- **The "Published but Blind" ICP is a pull sale, not a push sale.** These people already did the hard work. They shipped something. The pain is that they're flying blind — no analytics, no iteration signal, no usage data. The pitch is "you're giving away your expertise into a void." This is structurally different from every other experiment in the playbook because you're not convincing them to do something new; you're solving a problem they already have. Added as Experiment 7 with the YC investor as first test case.

- **The AI Enabler is a bottom-up path to Motion B.** The original Motion B activation requires top-down CTO outreach. The AI Enabler who deploys on Tokenrip for their department is the same consumerization-of-IT pattern as Slack/Dropbox/GitHub — bottom-up adoption that produces enterprise buy-intent. Doesn't change B's 2027 timeline (substrate still needs enterprise features) but suggests an acceleration path through organic D → B graduation.

- **"Power users" is a misleading label for the Glo population.** What looks like "power users" today will be standard worker behavior within 18 months. The distinction isn't technical skill — it's whether they build agents (builder) or mount agents others build (operator). Glo is an operator. The person who productizes Glo's ad-hoc workflows into a mountable agent is the builder. The AI Enabler role bridges this gap inside companies.

- **The YC investor demo should lead with blindness, not analytics.** "You published this skill. People use it. You have zero idea who, how often, or what they ask" is an emotional pitch about a real problem. "We add analytics" is a feature pitch. Different register. Lead with the pain, not the solution.

- **The ICP taxonomy is now a standalone reference doc.** Six ICPs organized by builder/operator split, mapped to motions and experiments. Lives at `bd/icps.md`. This is the reference for "who are we actually talking to" across all BD work.
