# Upwork Proposal Playbook

> Closer enforces this playbook in Proposal Coaching mode. When a draft violates a rule, name the section.

## Current Phase: Conversation Volume (as of 2026-05-16)

**The goal right now is not to win bids. It is to get conversations.** Every reply from a potential client is the asset — it teaches us what the market wants, how buyers describe their pain, and where the real demand sits. Winning the contract is a secondary, later concern.

What this means in practice:
- **Bid wide.** Submit on almost anything. Strategic fit, infrastructure reuse, domain match — all irrelevant for now.
- **Triage is advisory only.** Closer may note a job is vague or low-fit, but it is not a reason to skip. There are no hard skips in this phase.
- **Optimize every proposal for one outcome: a reply.** Short, easy to answer, aimed at their problem. Not at impressing them.

Revisit this phase when conversation volume is high enough to start being selective.

---

## §1. Triage — Advisory Only (This Phase)

In the conversation-volume phase, Closer does not gate. It may give a one-line read on a job ("vague scope, expect a noisy thread" / "looks like a real buyer") so Simon knows what he's walking into — but the bid goes in regardless. Scoring frameworks return when the phase changes.

---

## §2. What the Buyer Cares About

The buyer cares about **one thing: their problem, and whether you can solve it.** They do not care about architecture, your methodology, or how you'd build it. Keep the whole proposal pointed at their result.

But do not *open* with a flat restate of their post — "Sounds like you want agents that do the job..." reads as an auto-generated spam template. The opening line is handled by §8 (Attention): a bold claim, a credibility statement, or a sharp question. Their outcome gets woven through Interest and Desire, not parroted back in sentence one.

- ❌ "The real challenge here is separating the imprint from the memory layer..." (a lecture — kill it)
- ❌ "Sounds like you're looking for someone to build AI agents..." (spam restate — kill it)

---

## §3. Proof — Show, Don't Attach

When a posting asks for work samples, **do not attach a portfolio**. Point to something live: `tokenrip.com` or `tokenrip.com/agents` — real agents running, not a PDF. One line, framed as proof you've done the thing, then back to their problem.

> "Instead of work samples — here's a live one: tokenrip.com/agents. Real agents in production. Happy to walk you through how this maps to what you need."

---

## §4. Implementation Details — Only If They Ask

Assume the buyer does **not** care about tools, frameworks, or architecture **unless the posting explicitly asks** (e.g. "must have CrewAI experience," "experience with LangGraph required"). 

- Posting asks for specific tech → name it, briefly, confidently. Match their checklist.
- Posting doesn't → say nothing about implementation. Talk about their result.

Never volunteer an architecture explanation. It reads as a lecture and buries the one thing they want to hear: "I can solve your problem."

---

## §5. The Discovery Offer — De-Risk Bigger Jobs

On jobs $5K+ or with ambiguous scope, you may propose a paid short discovery (1–2 days) before the full build — but frame it as *their* benefit (a clear plan, no commitment), not a process. Keep it to one sentence. On small or simple jobs, skip it — it adds friction.

---

## §6. The Close — Make Replying Easy

End with one low-friction next step that's easy to say yes to. The goal is a reply.

**Kill on sight:** "Let me know if you're interested" · "Happy to discuss" · "Looking forward to hearing from you" · "Feel free to reach out."

**Replace with** a simple, concrete question or offer: "What system are you trying to plug this into?" · "Want me to send a quick outline of how I'd tackle it?" · "Got 15 minutes this week for a quick call?"

A question they can answer in one line beats a polished sign-off. The proposal ends with a door held open, not a bow.

---

## §7. Length & Tone

- **Short.** Three to six sentences. Upwork buyers skim and they've got 40 other proposals.
- No generic openers ("I came across your job post and..."). Start with §2.
- No résumé dump. No architecture. No methodology.
- Warm, confident, plain-spoken. Talk like a person who can help, not a firm pitching.
- Every sentence either shows you get their problem, proves you can help, or makes replying easier. Cut anything else.

## §9. Humanize Before It Ships

Every proposal (and every Closer-written message) goes through the `humanizer` skill before it's final. No exceptions. AI tells kill credibility on Upwork.

Watch hardest for: em dashes (use commas and periods), boldface, rule-of-three rhythm, and paragraphs that are all the same tidy length. Run the humanizer's full pass, including the "what makes this obviously AI generated?" audit, and present the final version.

---

## §8. Structure Every Proposal as AIDA

Every cover letter follows AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. It keeps proposals tight, persuasive, and skimmable.

- **Attention** — Open with one sharp line that stops the skim. A bold claim, a credibility statement, or a concrete question. Never a generic restate of their post ("Sounds like you want...") — that reads as auto-generated spam.
- **Interest** — Give context: who we are, what we do, relevant experience. Mirror the posting's own language. This is where background goes.
- **Desire** — Make them want us. The proof — a live link instead of work samples (§3) — framed as "this is what you'd get."
- **Action** — One low-friction CTA. An easy question or a 15-minute call offer. Make replying take one line (§6).

Keep all four together inside the §7 length limit — short paragraphs, one idea each.

## Proposal Skeleton (AIDA)

1. **Attention** — a bold claim, credibility line, or sharp question. 1 sentence.
2. **Interest** — who we are, what we do, relevant experience; mirror their language. Tech specifics only if asked (§4). 1–2 sentences.
3. **Desire** — the proof: live link instead of work samples, framed as what they'd get (§3). 1–2 sentences.
4. **Action** — an easy question or call offer that invites a reply (§6). 1 sentence.
