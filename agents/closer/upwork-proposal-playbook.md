# Upwork Proposal Playbook

> Closer enforces this playbook in Proposal Coaching mode. When a draft violates a rule, name the section.

## Current Phase: Conversation Volume (as of 2026-05-16)

**The goal right now is not to win bids. It is to get conversations.** Every reply from a potential client is the asset. It teaches us what the market wants, how buyers describe their pain, and where the real demand sits. Winning the contract is a secondary, later concern.

What this means in practice:
- **Bid wide.** Submit on almost anything. Strategic fit, infrastructure reuse, domain match are all irrelevant for now.
- **Triage is advisory only.** Closer may note a job is vague or low-fit, but it is not a reason to skip. There are no hard skips in this phase.
- **Optimize every proposal for one outcome: a reply.** Short, easy to answer, aimed at their problem. Not at impressing them.

Revisit this phase when conversation volume is high enough to start being selective.

---

## §1. Triage — Advisory Only (This Phase)

In the conversation-volume phase, Closer does not gate. It may give a one-line read on a job ("vague scope, expect a noisy thread" / "looks like a real buyer") so Simon knows what he's walking into, but the bid goes in regardless. Scoring frameworks return when the phase changes.

## §2. What Makes a Great Cover Letter

The client is skimming dozens of proposals, most of them generic templates, restated job posts, or résumé dumps. They are trying to answer four questions fast: is this a real person, do they get it, can they actually do it, and is replying low-risk. A great cover letter answers all four in three short paragraphs.

Seven principles:

1. **No ceremony.** Never open by narrating the post back ("you're looking for...", "sounds like you want...") or by naming what they requested ("you asked for..."). Both are throat-clearing. The first sentence should be something only a person who can actually do the job would say. See §3.
2. **Specificity is the proof of attention, not narration.** Reference the real particulars of the job — their stack, their tooling, their constraint — woven into normal sentences. Never announced as "you said X."
3. **Show competence, do not claim it.** Point to real work, or make a concrete useful move. Adjectives like "experienced" and "reliable" are worthless; every bidder writes them.
4. **One clickable proof beats a paragraph of claims.** A live thing they can open outweighs any description. See §4.
5. **Have a spine.** A genuine point of view, or a real way of working, makes you the memorable bidder. Bland loses even when qualified.
6. **Short, plain voice.** Three or four short paragraphs, signed with a name — judgment call on the fourth (a differentiator that needs its own beat, or an extra ask). Talk like a competent person who is interested, not a firm pitching.
7. **End with a real, low-risk, leading next step.** See §7.

The proposal is not a formula to fill in. It is a short, human message that proves you read the job, can do it, and are easy to start with.

---

## §3. Open on Substance — No Ceremony

The first sentence decides whether the rest gets read. It must **not** narrate the client's post back to them ("you're looking for...", "sounds like you want...", "you asked for...") and must **not** be a generic line about the bidder ("I'm an experienced developer...").

**Standard opener — inline the greeting.** Open with `Hi, <substance>` on a single line. Never put `Hi,` on its own line with a blank line under it: that burns two of the roughly six lines above the fold (§13) on nothing and pushes the proof out of the skim view. **Skip the self-introduction.** "I'm Simon" spends words the buyer doesn't need — the name is in the signature, and the winning bids lead with the work, not a name. The first words after "Hi," should be something only a person who can do the job would say.

If the client's name is visible, use it: `Hi [Name], <substance>`. It confirms the proposal isn't templated. On Upwork the name is usually hidden until interviewing, so `Hi,` is the default.

Three ways the first line can land (all inline, all straight to substance):

1. **Straight at what you do, concretely.** *"Hi, I build and run AI agents for a living, and most of what you listed is work I do day to day."* The relevance does the work — specific to this job, not a résumé line.
2. **A point of view that reframes the job.** *"Hi, most AI consulting is slideware. I'd rather point you at a system that's actually running:"* then the link. This has a spine; it answers the buyer's unspoken worry before they raise it.
3. **Lead at proof you can show.** *"Hi, I just recorded a 2-minute walkthrough of a lead-gen agent I built that does most of what you described:"* then the link. When Simon has a Loom or a live system that matches the job, opening at it is the strongest move — it puts verifiable proof above the fold (§13). Keep it honest: point to something real and clickable, never a manufactured metric (§11).

Each of these proves comprehension without narrating the post. The job's particulars still appear, woven into the body as normal sentences, never announced as "you said X."

**The "checked-understanding" open is retired.** The hedge ("if I'm reading this right, you want X") makes the bidder sound unsure of something an attentive reader would be sure of, and it is still ceremony. Skip it. If genuine ambiguity needs resolving, do it as a real question in the close (§7), not as a hedge in sentence one.

Two failure modes to keep killing:
- ❌ **Flat restate** — "Sounds like you're looking for someone to build AI agents..." Reads as a spam template.
- ❌ **Lecture** — "The real challenge here is separating X from Y..." Casts the bidder as teacher, the client as student. Presumptuous even when correct. The client is the buyer and the domain expert; the bidder is auditioning. Prove comprehension by being useful, never by reflecting the client's own problem back at them.

## §4. Proof — Show, Don't Attach

A live thing the buyer can open in ten seconds beats any paragraph of claims, and beats a static attachment — a clickable link removes doubt without a call. Lead the proof with whichever of these fits, and put it above the fold (§13):

- **A Loom / demo video, when Simon supplies one for this bid.** This is the single strongest differentiator on Upwork: a 2–3 minute screen recording of a real system, ideally one that maps to the posting. When Simon attaches a Loom (he'll flag it in his steer), it leads the proof and goes in the first few lines, framed as "I recorded a short walkthrough of X for you:". Never invent one; only include a link Simon actually gives.
- **`tokenrip.com`** — the platform itself, "a platform I built for running AI agents in production." Do **not** link `tokenrip.com/agents` in a cold proposal — it has only a couple of agents and invites the buyer to count them. The platform claim can't be weakened by an agent count; agent walkthroughs belong on the call.
- **`quintel.ai`** — Simon's document-heavy product build, a live link the buyer can open. Honesty bound (§11): it's v1/demo with mixed real and synthetic data; never described as having a paying or signed customer.

Both product links may appear in the same letter as verifiable prior work — high earners routinely give two or three live links. Leading with one credential (§12) governs the *framing* of the opener, not the number of links.

When a posting asks for work samples, point to these live rather than attaching a portfolio. If the posting asks for a blog or writing, the Tokenrip blog is a real asset — point to it as where the deeper thinking lives, rather than reproducing it in the letter.

Screenshots of Tokenrip and Quintel still attach as secondary proof (§8), but the clickable link is the primary move now: verifiable in seconds, and it survives the "Show more" fold.

## §5. Implementation Details — Only If They Ask

Assume the buyer does **not** care about tools, frameworks, or architecture **unless the posting explicitly asks** (e.g. "must have CrewAI experience," or a posted tech checklist).

- Posting names specific tech → name it back, briefly and confidently. Match their checklist; it signals no ramp-up.
- Posting doesn't → say nothing about implementation. Talk about their result.

Never volunteer an architecture explanation. It reads as a lecture and buries the one thing they want to hear: "I can solve your problem."

## §6. The Discovery Offer — De-Risk Bigger Jobs

On jobs $5K+ or with ambiguous scope, you may propose a paid short discovery (1–2 days) before the full build, but frame it as *their* benefit (a clear plan, no commitment), not a process. Keep it to one sentence. On small or simple jobs, skip it; it adds friction.

## §7. The Close — Make Replying Easy

End with one low-friction next step that's easy to say yes to. The goal is a reply.

**Kill on sight:** "Let me know if you're interested" · "Happy to discuss" · "Happy to hop on a call anytime" · "Looking forward to hearing from you" · "Feel free to reach out." These hand the burden to the client and put no time box on anything.

**Replace with** a concrete, leading next step that the bidder owns:
- An execution-forward question — "Which of these systems is most urgent right now?" — that advances toward the work and reads senior, not junior. Avoid detail questions a competent buyer finds obvious ("what format are the files in").
- A time-boxed call offer — "Got 15 minutes this week for a quick call?" — better still when you offer to bring something ("I'll come with a first-pass outline").

A question they can answer in one line beats a polished sign-off. The proposal ends with a door held open, not a bow.

**A warm call-offer is allowed as a secondary line (updated 2026-06-09).** The close still *leads* with the owned, leading next step (the execution-forward question). A friendly add-on after it is fine and reads human; Simon prefers it. The thing to keep killing is a call-offer that *is* the entire close (burden-shifting, no leading step before it).

**The standard call-offer line, just before the signoff.** End with a low-pressure offer to talk before any commitment — covering all three flavors in one breath: talk through requirements, run a short pre-discovery, or just check fit. Keep it to one sentence. A concise version that works:

> Happy to hop on a quick call first — talk through requirements, run a short pre-discovery, or just see if it's a fit before you commit to anything.

It sits after the leading next step and before `Thanks,`. Vary the wording per bid so it doesn't read as boilerplate, but keep the three options and the "before you commit to anything" reassurance — that's the part that lowers the buyer's risk of replying.

**Standard signature + LinkedIn line.** Close the letter with `Thanks,` on its own line, then `Simon`. After the signature, append one final line, verbatim:

> P.S. - Feel free to look me up on LinkedIn for more information: Simon Pettibone

It's a low-friction trust signal, a real name and a real person, easily verifiable. The "P.S." reads personal and human, which is the point. Goes on every cover letter. (Leave the hyphen as a hyphen — never let the humanizer convert it to an em dash, §9.)

## §8. Length, Structure & Tone

Three short paragraphs as the spine, signed with a name — three moves, not four ceremonial stages:

1. **Open on substance** (§3) — who you are or your point of view, concretely.
2. **Show you can do it** — relevant real experience, the job's particulars woven in, one clickable proof (§4, §5), and the one differentiator that fits the job below the fold (§14).
3. **Close** (§7) — one leading, low-risk next step.

- A fourth short paragraph is a judgment call, not a violation. When a differentiator (§14) needs its own beat below the fold, or the posting explicitly asks for several things, give it a paragraph — but each one must do real work (answer an ask, land a differentiator), never pad. The enemy is ceremony and the résumé dump, not the paragraph count.
- It's a cover letter, not a text message. Complete sentences, warm, confident, plain-spoken. Talk like a person who can help, not a firm pitching.
- No generic openers ("I came across your job post and..."). No résumé dump. No architecture or methodology (§5).
- Every sentence either shows you get their problem, proves you can help, or makes replying easier. Cut anything else.
- Buyers skim. Every line earns its place; filler is the enemy, not length.
- **Keep the hero tight; use the room you earn below the fold.** The first six lines (§13) are ruthlessly tight — that's what wins the open. Below the fold, a relevant supporting paragraph or a fitting differentiator (§14) earns its place. Aim for 150–300 words total. The 600+ word résumé dump is what reads template-heavy and loses — not length that's actually doing work. If a sentence isn't hooking, proving, differentiating, or making the reply easier, cut it.

**Voice — write like Simon, not like a strategist (added 2026-06-09, Simon's direct feedback).**
- **Use contractions liberally.** "I'll," "you're," "that's," "I'd," "it's." Reads friendly and human-written, not like a memo. This is the single biggest tell that a person wrote it. Write the way Simon talks, not the way a strategist writes.
- **Cut the high-horse pushback paragraph.** The standalone "one piece of senior advice up front..." sermon (telling the buyer how teams get burned, what the disciplined path is) is *not effective* and reads as preachy. A genuine point of view (§3, principle 5) belongs in **one compressed sentence**, not a paragraph that lectures. When in doubt, cut it and get to the point.
- **Get to the point.** Shorter beats more. The opener names the hard part and the close asks the question; everything between is the minimum proof, nothing more.
- **Lean on attachments now.** Simon attaches screenshots of Tokenrip and Quintel to relevant bids. Reference them lightly ("see portfolio + attached file") instead of describing the proof in prose. The picture carries the weight the paragraph used to.

## §9. Humanize Before It Ships

Every proposal (and every Closer-written message) goes through the `humanizer` skill before it's final. No exceptions. AI tells kill credibility on Upwork.

Watch hardest for: em dashes (use commas and periods), boldface, rule-of-three rhythm, and paragraphs that are all the same tidy length. Run the humanizer's full pass, including the "what makes this obviously AI generated?" audit, and present the final version.

Keep genuine enumerations that mirror the buyer's posted checklist (§5) — those read as attention, not as AI rhythm. The rule-of-three to kill is the *rhetorical* triad ("fast, reliable, and scalable"), not a real list of their five named tools.

## §10. Pricing & Milestone Breakdowns

Many postings include a milestone field and ask for a breakdown. The breakdown is where pricing discipline shows. The full pricing library is in `memory/patterns.md`; the rules that govern an Upwork bid:

- **De-risk comes from structure, not a low price.** A milestone sequence that proves value early beats a cheap number. Never compete on being cheapest. On a sophisticated buyer (detailed spec, "reliability over a demo"), the cheap bid reads as junior and *loses* — the serious price with a smart structure signals you're the real one. Price seriously, structure tightly.
- **The riskiest-scope milestone is never the cheapest.** The most uncertain work (open-ended scraping, an unproven accuracy bar, undefined document messiness) must not be priced as a throwaway. The cheapest milestone should be the lowest-risk one: docs, handover, polish.
- **No hunger tells.** No $0 milestones, no free proof-of-concept, no trivially cheap "demo" rung. Every milestone delivers real value at a real price. A giveaway to seem eager reads junior.
- **Milestone 1 as paid discovery — the default for ambiguous or $5K+ jobs.** When scope is underspecified or can't be quoted blind, make M1 a paid discovery that ships a real deliverable: define the scope/schema with them, prove the core on a slice of their real data, and come out with a confirmed architecture and price for the rest. It de-risks them (they see something working before committing the full budget) and de-risks you (you never fixed-price against unknowns). It is paid and substantial, not a freebie. Confirm later milestones at the end of M1 once scope is settled, and say so honestly.
- **Undefined scope → one discovery milestone, not a fabricated plan.** When the posting is genuinely open ("idea to production," no deliverables list), do not invent a tidy four-milestone plan. A single paid-discovery milestone that picks the highest-pain wedge and takes it live is more honest and reads more senior. A buyer who said "no demos" will smell a fake breakdown.
- **Land-and-expand.** Price the first milestone for close-rate (often the highest-value, easy-yes deliverable) and defer the rest. When the posting itself promises ongoing work, don't pitch it; nail the first milestone and the relationship opens.
- **The breakdown goes in the milestone fields, not the letter.** The cover letter gets one line ("I've put a milestone breakdown in the milestones"); the detail lives in the platform's milestone fields. Keep the letter short.
- **Each milestone description must be under 200 characters.** Upwork hard-caps the milestone field. Write each description tight enough to fit, and verify the character count before presenting. Lead with the phase's purpose, then a parenthetical "e.g." of examples; cut articles and connective words before cutting meaning.
- **Generalize milestones to the phase's purpose, not a fixed deliverable list, when the spec isn't in hand yet.** Before the full spec lands, name what each phase is *for* (runtime + integration, core behavior, tooling, hardening/validation) and flag specific deliverables as examples ("e.g.") deferred to the spec. Don't commit to a deliverable (a specific briefing cadence, an exact interface) the posting hasn't confirmed.
- **Anchor the total honestly.** Pick a reference total that matches the real scope and signals senior, then note it's a reference, scalable to their budget signals. State a number; let it set expectations.

## §11. Honesty & Confidentiality Guardrails

Every claim must survive a skeptical buyer clicking through, and every client reference must respect confidentiality.

**Confidential engagements — industry abstraction only.** The equipment-finance and healthcare-credentialing engagements are confidential. Reference them at the industry level, never by name:
- Equipment finance → "a company in equipment finance" / "the equipment finance space." Never name the client, the partner, or any cluster node.
- Healthcare credentialing → "a regulated, document-heavy workflow in healthcare credentialing." Never name the client.
- Never disclose deal specifics, PII, or partner identities in any external artifact.

**Own products are nameable, within limits.**
- **quintel.ai** — Simon's product; may be named and shown (portfolio, screenshots). It is v1/demo with mixed real and synthetic data. Do **not** describe it as having a signed or paying customer, or as running live production deals.
- **Tokenrip** — nameable; "a platform / memory layer I built for running AI agents in production." Don't link tokenrip.com/agents in a cold proposal (§4).

**The honesty checkpoint before sending.** Any framework or stack claim Simon can't back hands-on is a credibility risk to a buyer who researched the tools. For each named technology, ask: shipped it, or just *could*? If a claim is thinner than the rest, soften it to a capability ("models served on your own GPUs") rather than a specific tool ("vLLM with quantization"), or let the discovery milestone make it the thing you *measure* rather than assert. Never state an unverifiable specific — a deprecation date, an accuracy number you haven't measured on their data.

## §12. Lead With the Right Proof

Same person, different lead. Match the opening credential to what the posting names as the hard part:

- **Memory / persistence / "consistent, not forgetful" / context across sessions** → lead with **Tokenrip**, the memory and shared-context layer. Attach the Tokenrip screenshot.
- **Document extraction / validation / RAG / structured data / human-in-the-loop / eval** → lead with the **healthcare-credentialing extraction engine** (abstracted): the extract → validate → route-low-confidence-to-a-human system in progress now.
- **Full product build / "idea to production" / agents over internal docs / ops automation** → lead with the **production-system** framing plus **quintel.ai** as the document-heavy build reference; name the stack only if the posting asks (§5).

Attach the screenshot that matches the lead (Tokenrip for memory/agent-infra, Quintel for document/full-build), and use the live link as the primary proof (§4, §13). The matching picture carries proof the prose used to. Lead with one credential — the closest-fit one; the sharpest single match beats a résumé of all of them. This governs the opening *framing*, not the link count: a second live link (the other product) is fine as verifiable prior work, but only one credential frames the letter.

## §13. The First Six Lines Are the Hero

Upwork truncates the cover letter in the client's list view after roughly six lines, then collapses the rest behind "Show more." Simon confirmed this from the poster's own view: six lines is what the buyer sees before deciding whether to expand. Those six lines are the hero, and their only job is to earn the open. Lead with the attention-getters; the rest of the case can wait for below the fold.

**The hero (first six lines) carries the hook:**
- **Inline the greeting (§3).** Never spend a line on `Hi,` by itself plus a blank line under it; `Hi, <substance>` keeps both working. Skip the self-introduction up here — every hero line is scarce.
- **Put the strongest clickable proof in the first few lines.** A Loom link, `tokenrip.com`, or `quintel.ai` belongs near the top, not in a closing "here's my portfolio" paragraph. Two winning patterns from real bids on Simon's own posting: one opened *"Hey Simon, just recorded this 2 min video mapping it out for you:"* with the Loom link on the next line; another led its second sentence with *"A recent project I built was for [client] (https://…)."* The live link was above the fold in both.
- **Front-load, don't bury.** Order: one substance sentence, then the proof link. Ceremony and résumé never earn a hero line.

**Below the fold is still usable real estate.** Once the hero earns the open, the reader is engaged — and that's where the supporting case lives: the relevant experience, the one differentiator that fits the job (§14), the close (§7). Don't cram the differentiators into the hero; let them do their work below the fold, where there's room. More than six lines is fine. The constraint is that the *first* six have to hook, not that the whole proposal fit in six.

## §14. Differentiators — The Below-the-Fold Case

Once the hero (§13) has earned the open, these are what close the case. They live below the fold, not in the first six lines. Pick the one (occasionally two) that genuinely fits the posting — judgment based on the job, not a checklist to stack. Each is a real edge Simon has; the job is to deploy the right one, not all of them.

- **Ownership, not dependency.** A strong differentiator for bootstrapped buyers wary of vendor lock-in: Simon hands off something the customer owns and can run themselves — on their infrastructure, their keys — not a system that needs him to babysit it. Frame it as their benefit: they own 100% at handoff, their own team can maintain it without calling an engineer. This mirrors the forward-deployed-engineer thesis (sell ownership, not dependency; the follow-on work comes anyway, on their terms). Use it when the posting hints at long-term-dependence worries, internal tooling, or "we want to own this."
- **Full-stack range.** The differentiator against someone who just discovered AI automation: Simon comes from a full-stack engineering background, so the range runs from a simple workflow automation all the way to an enterprise full-stack application that integrates with existing systems or is built to be extended. Use it when the posting implies integration with existing systems, a real product (not a toy workflow), extensibility, or enterprise scope. Skip it on a small, self-contained automation where it reads as overkill.
- **US-based, real-time feedback loop.** When the posting signals collaboration, iterative work, or a build that benefits from same-day turnaround, lead the availability angle: based in the US, available any time, can hop on a call quickly. The pitch is a tight feedback loop — present the system in real time, get feedback in real time, even build together ("dual build") rather than the full-day round-trip of an offshore contractor. Default-assume the posting is US-based; Simon removes this line himself if it isn't. Skip it on simple one-off automations where async is fine.

These earn their place by fitting the job, not by length. A proposal can run past the hero into a tight supporting paragraph or two and still read concise. What kills credibility is the generic résumé dump (§8), not a relevant differentiator the buyer actually cares about.
