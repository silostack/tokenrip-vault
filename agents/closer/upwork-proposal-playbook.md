# Upwork Proposal Playbook

> Closer enforces this playbook in Proposal Coaching mode. When a draft violates a rule, name the section.

## Current Phase: Conversation Volume (as of 2026-05-16)

**The goal right now is not to win bids. It is to get conversations.** Every reply from a potential client is the asset. It teaches us what the market wants, how buyers describe their pain, and where the real demand sits. Winning the contract is a secondary, later concern.

What this means in practice:
- **Bid wide.** Submit on almost anything. Strategic fit, infrastructure reuse, domain match are all irrelevant for now.
- **Triage is advisory only.** Closer may note a job is vague or low-fit, but it is not a reason to skip. There are no hard skips in this phase.
- **Optimize every proposal for one outcome: a reply.** Short, easy to answer, aimed at their problem. Not at impressing them.

Revisit this phase when conversation volume is high enough to start being selective.

---

## §1. Triage — Advisory Only (This Phase)

In the conversation-volume phase, Closer does not gate. It may give a one-line read on a job ("vague scope, expect a noisy thread" / "looks like a real buyer") so Simon knows what he's walking into, but the bid goes in regardless. Scoring frameworks return when the phase changes.

## §2. What Makes a Great Cover Letter

The client is skimming dozens of proposals, most of them generic templates, restated job posts, or résumé dumps. They are trying to answer four questions fast: is this a real person, do they get it, can they actually do it, and is replying low-risk. A great cover letter answers all four in three short paragraphs.

Seven principles:

1. **No ceremony.** Never open by narrating the post back ("you're looking for...", "sounds like you want...") or by naming what they requested ("you asked for..."). Both are throat-clearing. The first sentence should be something only a person who can actually do the job would say. See §3.
2. **Specificity is the proof of attention, not narration.** Reference the real particulars of the job — their stack, their tooling, their constraint — woven into normal sentences. Never announced as "you said X."
3. **Show competence, do not claim it.** Point to real work, or make a concrete useful move. Adjectives like "experienced" and "reliable" are worthless; every bidder writes them.
4. **One clickable proof beats a paragraph of claims.** A live thing they can open outweighs any description. See §4.
5. **Have a spine.** A genuine point of view, or a real way of working, makes you the memorable bidder. Bland loses even when qualified.
6. **Short, plain voice.** Three short paragraphs, signed with a name. Talk like a competent person who is interested, not a firm pitching.
7. **End with a real, low-risk, leading next step.** See §7.

The proposal is not a formula to fill in. It is a short, human message that proves you read the job, can do it, and are easy to start with.

---

## §3. Open on Substance — No Ceremony

The first sentence decides whether the rest gets read. It must **not** narrate the client's post back to them ("you're looking for...", "sounds like you want...", "you asked for...") and must **not** be a generic line about the bidder ("I'm an experienced developer...").

Open with substance. Two openings that work:

1. **Who you are, in one concrete, relevant sentence.** *"I build and run AI agents for a living, and most of what you listed is work I do day to day."* The relevance does the work — it is specific to this job, not a résumé line.
2. **A real point of view that reframes the job.** *"Most AI consulting is slideware. I'd rather point you at a system that's actually running."* This has a spine; it answers the buyer's unspoken worry before they raise it.

Both prove comprehension without narrating the post. The particulars of the job still appear, woven into the body as normal sentences, never announced as "you said X."

**The "checked-understanding" open is retired.** The hedge ("if I'm reading this right, you want X") makes the bidder sound unsure of something an attentive reader would be sure of, and it is still ceremony. Skip it. If genuine ambiguity needs resolving, do it as a real question in the close (§7), not as a hedge in sentence one.

Two failure modes to keep killing:
- ❌ **Flat restate** — "Sounds like you're looking for someone to build AI agents..." Reads as a spam template.
- ❌ **Lecture** — "The real challenge here is separating X from Y..." Casts the bidder as teacher, the client as student. Presumptuous even when correct. The client is the buyer and the domain expert; the bidder is auditioning. Prove comprehension by being useful, never by reflecting the client's own problem back at them.

---

## §4. Proof — Show, Don't Attach

When a posting asks for work samples, **do not attach a portfolio**. Point to something live: `tokenrip.com`, the platform itself. One line, framed as proof you have built and run the real thing, then back to their problem.

Do **not** link `tokenrip.com/agents` in a cold proposal — it has only a couple of agents and invites the buyer to count them. The platform claim ("a platform I built for running AI agents in production") is stronger and cannot be weakened by an agent count. Agent walkthroughs belong on the call.

If the posting asks for a blog or writing, the Tokenrip blog is a real asset — point to it as where the deeper thinking lives, rather than reproducing it in the letter.

---

## §5. Implementation Details — Only If They Ask

Assume the buyer does **not** care about tools, frameworks, or architecture **unless the posting explicitly asks** (e.g. "must have CrewAI experience," or a posted tech checklist).

- Posting names specific tech → name it back, briefly and confidently. Match their checklist; it signals no ramp-up.
- Posting doesn't → say nothing about implementation. Talk about their result.

Never volunteer an architecture explanation. It reads as a lecture and buries the one thing they want to hear: "I can solve your problem."

---

## §6. The Discovery Offer — De-Risk Bigger Jobs

On jobs $5K+ or with ambiguous scope, you may propose a paid short discovery (1–2 days) before the full build, but frame it as *their* benefit (a clear plan, no commitment), not a process. Keep it to one sentence. On small or simple jobs, skip it; it adds friction.

---

## §7. The Close — Make Replying Easy

End with one low-friction next step that's easy to say yes to. The goal is a reply.

**Kill on sight:** "Let me know if you're interested" · "Happy to discuss" · "Happy to hop on a call anytime" · "Looking forward to hearing from you" · "Feel free to reach out." These hand the burden to the client and put no time box on anything.

**Replace with** a concrete, leading next step that the bidder owns:
- An execution-forward question — "Which of these systems is most urgent right now?" — that advances toward the work and reads senior, not junior. Avoid detail questions a competent buyer finds obvious ("what format are the files in").
- A time-boxed call offer — "Got 15 minutes this week for a quick call?" — better still when you offer to bring something ("I'll come with a first-pass outline").

A question they can answer in one line beats a polished sign-off. The proposal ends with a door held open, not a bow.

---

## §8. Length, Structure & Tone

Three short paragraphs, signed with a name. Not four ceremonial stages — three moves:

1. **Open on substance** (§3) — who you are or your point of view, concretely.
2. **Show you can do it** — relevant real experience, the job's particulars woven in, and one clickable proof (§4, §5).
3. **Close** (§7) — one leading, low-risk next step.

- Longer than three paragraphs is allowed **only** when the posting explicitly asks for several things; then each extra paragraph answers one ask, and nothing else.
- It's a cover letter, not a text message. Complete sentences, warm, confident, plain-spoken. Talk like a person who can help, not a firm pitching.
- No generic openers ("I came across your job post and..."). No résumé dump. No architecture or methodology (§5).
- Every sentence either shows you get their problem, proves you can help, or makes replying easier. Cut anything else.
- Buyers skim. Every line earns its place; filler is the enemy, not length.

---

## §9. Humanize Before It Ships

Every proposal (and every Closer-written message) goes through the `humanizer` skill before it's final. No exceptions. AI tells kill credibility on Upwork.

Watch hardest for: em dashes (use commas and periods), boldface, rule-of-three rhythm, and paragraphs that are all the same tidy length. Run the humanizer's full pass, including the "what makes this obviously AI generated?" audit, and present the final version.
