## What We're Doing and Why

**Current state.** Tokenrip platform is deployed and live. CLI and public docs are up. The Chief of Staff agent is in active build (the lighthouse mounted-agent imprint shipping the architecture). The engagement agent is operational.

**Active motion.** Audience-led creator deployment — sell mounted-agent infrastructure to experts and creators with engaged AI-adjacent audiences (50k-500k followers across YouTube, Twitter/X, Substack, newsletters, podcasts, LinkedIn). Each creator publishes a personal-brand imprint on Tokenrip. Their audience mounts the imprint in their preferred runtime. The audience pays for their own inference. The creator collects on tooling-tier upgrades from their audience's engagement.

**Eventual goal.** Substrate density — registered operators, published imprints, accumulated shared memory, weekly tool calls — that enables Motion B (vendor substrate license sales) in 2027 and Motion C (inter-agent marketplace) in 2027-2028. See [[motions-and-strategy]] for the full progression rationale.

**The compounding curve.** Each creator deploy adds an imprint to the asset graph + registers their audience as Tokenrip operators + accumulates shared memory across all users of that imprint. The substrate gets denser with every deploy. Twenty mid-tier creators averaging 150k audience size at a 3% mount rate = 90,000 registered operators. That is the curve fundraising sells against.

**Why this motion specifically.** Every layer of the mounted-agent architecture is load-bearing for a creator-with-audience imprint. No other motion passes the architectural-requirement test as cleanly. Cross-reference: [[motions-and-strategy]] section "Motion E: Audience-Led Creator Deployment."

### Hero vs. Mid-Tier — Why Mid-Tier Is the Right Primary Focus

| Dimension | Hero (Garry, Lenny, Naval) | Mid-tier (50k-500k AI creators) |
|---|---|---|
| Time to first deploy | 3-6 months | 1-2 weeks |
| Outreach hit rate | <5% | 20-40% |
| Audience per deploy | 500k-2M | 50k-300k |
| Distribution model | High but single shot | Compounding across many |
| Incentive alignment | Weak (they don't need you) | Strong (you give them new monetization) |
| Exclusivity demand | High | Low |
| Effort per relationship | Founder-grade | Repeatable / scalable |
| Failure cost | Public loss | Quiet pivot |

The hero-tier creator is structurally better positioned to be the marketing artifact you point at *after* you have substrate density. The mid-tier crowd is structurally better positioned to be your first 25 imprints.

**Distribution math:** 20 mid-tier × 150k average audience × 3% mount rate = 90,000 registered operators. 1 hero × 2M audience × 1% mount rate = 20,000 registered operators. Volume + diversity wins on substrate density. The hero wins on press.

The roles reinforce: **hero = marketing. Mid-tier = substrate. Long-tail = marketplace seed.**

### Long-Tail Builders

Independent agent builders without an audience but with real use cases. Lower distribution per imprint but they're the seed of the marketplace. Funnel: Series 3 blog → Tokenrip docs → tooling-tier signup. Active sales motion is not necessary; passive distribution through content is. See [[motions-and-strategy]] "Motion D: Builder-Direct" for full detail.

## Creator Categories with Named Seed Candidates

Seven buckets. Each has its own pitch shape, deal-structure variant, and outreach channel. Named candidates below are seeds to unstick day-1 list-building — Alek's job is to expand each bucket to 25-50 vetted candidates.

### 1. AI Educators on YouTube (10k-300k subs)

**Audience profile.** AI-curious technical and semi-technical viewers. Already paying for inference. Already mounting Custom GPTs / Claude Projects.

**Monetization pain.** Ad / sponsorship revenue caps. Course launches are episodic and high-effort. Want recurring owned monetization.

**Pitch shape.** "Your audience already mounts agents from creators they trust — yours doesn't exist yet. Ship a [your-domain] agent with your methodology and they mount it instead of a generic Custom GPT. Recurring revenue from tooling tiers."

**Deal variant.** Free white-glove deploy + revenue share on tooling-tier upgrades. Co-marketing post-launch (creator features the agent in 2-3 videos; Tokenrip features the creator on the marketplace landing page).

**Channel.** YouTube DM if open, otherwise email through their public channel email. Twitter/X DM is often the highest-response channel for this group.

**Seed candidates.** Wes Roth, Matt Wolfe, McKay Wrigley, Riley Brown, Nate B Jones, AI Explained, David Shapiro. Plus the long tail of <100k subscriber AI tutorial channels — these often have the highest engagement per subscriber.

### 2. AI Newsletters (5k-100k subs)

**Audience profile.** Highly engaged, willing to pay (most AI newsletter audiences convert at higher rates than horizontal newsletter audiences).

**Monetization pain.** Paid newsletter conversion is hard. Subscribers want depth that takes time to produce. Sponsorships dilute editorial.

**Pitch shape.** "Paid newsletter conversions plateau at ~5%. An agent imprint based on your analysis methodology is the upgrade your top-tier subscribers want — and it learns from how they use it, which becomes your next newsletter angle."

**Deal variant.** Same as YouTube: free white-glove deploy + revenue share + co-marketing in newsletter editions. Bonus: the imprint produces market-intelligence data (what queries audience asks) that's directly useful for the newsletter itself.

**Channel.** Email reply to a recent newsletter edition is the best inbound — newsletter writers read replies.

**Seed candidates.** Ben's Bites alumni, The Rundown, TLDR AI, AI Breakfast, Superhuman, Last Week in AI, plus niche AI newsletters in legal/medical/marketing/dev verticals.

### 3. AI Twitter/X Personalities (50k-500k followers)

**Audience profile.** Builder-adjacent, engagement-rich, often early adopters of new agent tools.

**Monetization pain.** Zero monetization on raw social presence. Course is the standard play but high-friction. Substack would dilute Twitter-native voice.

**Pitch shape.** "Twitter is unmonetizable except via courses or consulting. An agent imprint with your name on it is the first real product you can ship without leaving Twitter. We do the deploy. You announce."

**Deal variant.** Same plus a Twitter co-launch protocol (creator pre-tweets, deploy day tweet thread, Tokenrip RT and amplification).

**Channel.** Twitter/X DM is native. Quote-tweet engagement before DM warms it up.

**Seed candidates.** Linus, Bilawal Sidhu, McKay Wrigley (cross-platform with YouTube), Rowan Cheung, Sully Omar, Logan Kilpatrick (former OpenAI DevRel). Plus the prompt-engineering and agent-builder Twitter set — search for accounts posting regularly about Claude Code, Cursor agents, Custom GPTs.

### 4. AI-Vertical Experts (audience varies, high engagement)

**Audience profile.** Domain-specific (legal AI, medical AI, marketing AI, sales AI). Often smaller follower counts but extremely high engagement and high willingness to pay for depth.

**Monetization pain.** Their expertise is in their head. Consulting doesn't scale. Courses get pirated. Books take a year.

**Pitch shape.** "You teach lawyers how to use AI in research. Your students would mount a 'how does [creator name] approach AI legal research' agent for $50/month tooling tier. We package your methodology as the imprint. Your students pay their own model."

**Deal variant.** May warrant higher revenue-share percentage given vertical depth. Possibly a vertical-specific landing page on the marketplace.

**Channel.** LinkedIn, vertical-specific community Slacks, conference speaker rosters.

**Seed candidates.** Marketing AI specialists (search for "AI marketing strategist" with active newsletters), legal AI specialists (Legal Tech Twitter circuit), sales AI specialists (Sales-AI tooling reviewers), AI-for-X niche owners. Specific names per vertical to be sourced by Alek.

### 5. LinkedIn AI Thought Leaders (50k-200k followers, side consulting)

**Audience profile.** Corporate AI advisor crowd. Consultants with active posting habits. Often have 50k-200k LinkedIn followers + side consulting practice.

**Monetization pain.** Consulting doesn't scale. Want to productize methodology without leaving the consulting motion.

**Pitch shape.** "Your consulting clients want your methodology in their own tools, but you can't be in 50 places at once. An agent imprint of your methodology = continuous client value without your time. We build it. You retain the relationship."

**Deal variant.** B2B-leaning. Possibly multiple imprints per consultant (one per methodology). Higher revenue share if creator is willing to commit to consistent maintenance.

**Channel.** LinkedIn message; introduction through mutual connection if warm path available.

**Seed candidates.** Allie K. Miller, Bernard Marr, the corporate AI advisor crowd posting substantively (not pure thought-leadership grift). Filter for: substantive technical posts, demonstrated client work, engagement that includes practitioners.

### 6. Substack AI Writers (paid AI Substacks)

**Audience profile.** Paid Substack subscribers — highly committed, intellectually engaged.

**Monetization pain.** Paid Substack growth plateaus at the ~5% conversion ceiling. Power users want a higher tier.

**Pitch shape.** "Paid Substack tops out around 5% conversion. An agent imprint of your analytical framework is the higher-tier offering — your Patreon, but actually useful daily. Audience already pays you for analysis; this is recurring analysis-as-a-service."

**Deal variant.** Same revenue-share structure; possibly a discount for existing paid subscribers as audience-acquisition driver.

**Channel.** Substack reply-to-post; Twitter DM if writer is active there.

**Seed candidates.** Paid AI Substacks (Stratechery AI coverage adjacents, niche paid AI Substacks like Charlie Warzel/Casey Newton AI angles, AI vertical Substacks). Also: writers who recently launched AI-specific Substacks within the last 12 months (often most receptive to new monetization paths).

### 7. AI Podcasters (mid-tier, not Lex)

**Audience profile.** Audio-first AI-curious audience. Often power users of agent tools.

**Monetization pain.** Ad-supported podcasting is brutal at sub-Lex scales. Sponsorship inventory is finite. Audience wants more depth between episodes.

**Pitch shape.** "Your audience listens for an hour a week and would use an agent every day if it sounded like you. Imprint your interviewing/analysis style; audience pays for their own model use. Recurring revenue not tied to ad inventory."

**Deal variant.** Often paired with a podcast-guest spot trade (Simon goes on the podcast, creator gets the deploy). Co-marketing through the podcast itself.

**Channel.** Podcast guest pitch as warm-up; LinkedIn or email follow-through.

**Seed candidates.** Cognitive Revolution, This Week in Startups (AI episodes), Latent Space, Hard Fork (probably too big), plus smaller niche AI podcasts. Filter for: active podcast in last 30 days, ~10k+ downloads per episode, host is the editorial voice (not just an interviewer).

### Pitch Framings: Two Paths to Yes

Most creators considering a Tokenrip imprint fit one of two framings, depending on their existing monetization shape. Choose the framing that fits where the creator already is — not where it would be convenient for Tokenrip if they were.

**Framing A — Tooling-Tier Monetization (default).** For creators *without* an existing high-ticket value ladder. The imprint is their first real product; tooling-tier revenue is the new monetization channel.

- Pitch: "Your audience pays for their own model usage. Tokenrip charges them for advanced tooling features. You get revenue share."
- Best fit: YouTube creators without courses, Twitter personalities, AI Substack writers without paid tiers, podcasters monetized only by ads/sponsorships.
- Deal structure: standard 30-50% rev share on tooling tiers.
- Creator's incremental work: imprint deployment + co-marketing.
- Tokenrip captures: tooling-tier revenue (with rev share to creator).

**Framing B — Funnel Upsell (for creators with existing value ladders).** For creators *with* an existing book / course / consulting / coaching ladder. The imprint is the interactive lead magnet inside their existing funnel — demonstrates the methodology in the operator's own context, surfaces the gaps that the deeper offerings address, drives qualified leads to their existing ladder.

- Pitch: "Your audience reads your methodology but never tries it in their own context. The imprint is the interactive piece that makes your existing funnel work harder — demonstrates value, surfaces the gaps your book/course/consulting fills, qualifies leads. The book and course become natural upsells from inside a working agent, not just downloads from a static page."
- Best fit: LinkedIn AI thought leaders with consulting practice, newsletter writers with courses, Substack writers with cohort programs, vertical experts with books, methodology owners running email drips into upsells (e.g. GTM Strategist).
- Deal structure: lower rev share on tooling tiers (or none); creator's existing funnel captures the upside. Tokenrip's value is substrate density (operators, memory, usage data) and category formation, not direct revenue from this creator.
- Creator's incremental work: imprint deployment + integration into existing funnel content (how the agent appears in newsletters, course materials, drip sequences).
- Tokenrip captures: substrate density. Creator captures: their existing ladder revenue, potentially uplifted by qualified leads.

**Why both framings matter for Tokenrip.** Framing A is direct revenue. Framing B is substrate density — and substrate density is what the fundraising curve sells against. A creator running Framing B may convert into Framing A later (as tooling tiers become useful for their audience), but the initial yes is structurally easier when the deal doesn't ask them to learn a new monetization model.

For value-ladder creators specifically, Framing A often *blocks* the conversation ("I don't want to figure out a new revenue stream; I already have a course business"). Framing B unblocks it ("the agent makes your existing course business work better"). Same imprint, same architecture, different commercial framing.

**How to choose the framing.**

| Signal in creator's profile | Framing |
|---|---|
| No book / course / consulting; monetizes through ads, sponsorships, or nothing yet | A |
| Book + course + consulting ladder; runs email drips with upsells; sells methodology | B |
| Both: existing ladder + audience large enough to support tooling tiers | Both — start with B (easier yes), add A as the relationship deepens |

The trap to avoid: leading with Framing A on a creator who is clearly Framing B. They'll hear "we're asking you to learn new monetization" and decline on principle. Read the creator's existing funnel before sending the pitch. The skill or content library they've published is usually the tell — if it sits inside an obvious book/course funnel, Framing B is the right opener.

## Deal Structures

**White-glove deployment.** Free initial build for the first 25 mid-tier deploys. Roughly 2 weeks of Simon (and team, post-hire) time per imprint. The white-glove cost is paid back through revenue share over 12+ months and through marketing leverage (each deploy is a public proof point).

**Revenue share on tooling tiers.** Audience members upgrade to paid tooling tiers (semantic search over the imprint's memory, advanced features). Revenue share with the creator. Concrete percentage and tier structure to be locked in next-session deep-dive — proposed range: 30-50% to creator depending on tier and exclusivity.

**Exclusivity rules.**
- Mid-tier: **no exclusivity.** Multiple creators in the same category is the explicit goal (substrate density requires diverse imprints). Refuse exclusivity demands at this tier.
- Hero: **up to 12 months** with minimum performance commitments (creator must promote, post-deploy minimum audience reach commitments). Exclusivity in narrow scope (e.g., "the only VC office hours imprint" rather than "the only AI imprint").

**Cash advance for hero-tier (post-capital only).** If a hero creator wants a meaningful upfront commitment, advance against future revenue share — only after capital lands. Pre-capital, hero deals are co-marketing-only.

**Co-marketing terms.** Creator promotes the deploy on their channel (specific commitments per channel: ≥3 mentions for YouTube, ≥1 newsletter feature, ≥5 tweets, etc.). Tokenrip features the creator on the marketplace landing page, in the Tokenrip newsletter, on Twitter, in case-study content.

**IP retention.** Creator owns their imprint content (their methodology, voice, instructions). Tokenrip retains substrate IP (the platform itself). Creator can fork the imprint and run it elsewhere — but if they do, shared memory accumulated on Tokenrip stays with the canonical imprint (per fork semantics in [[../product/tokenrip/mounted-agent-synthesis]]).

**Term sheet template.** Concrete v1 to be drafted in next-session deep-dive. Skeleton:
- Parties
- Imprint scope and ownership
- Revenue share structure
- Exclusivity terms (mid-tier: none; hero: scoped + time-bound)
- Co-marketing commitments
- IP retention
- Termination + portability
- Dispute resolution

## Deployment Process

How a creator's imprint actually gets built and shipped:

### Phase 1: Discovery (week 1)

- 60-90 minute interview with the creator. Their methodology. Their voice. What questions they get asked most. What they say no to.
- Ingest their existing content: 10-20 representative videos / newsletters / posts / podcast episodes. Pattern-extract methodology.
- Identify imprint structure: persona, methodology skill(s), response patterns, voice guidelines, edge cases.

### Phase 2: Packaging (week 1-2)

- Structure as Tokenrip imprint assets (versioned, fetchable). Reference implementation: Chief of Staff.
- Set up shared memory layer (anonymized patterns across audience users) and private context layer (per-operator history).
- Generate the bootloader command file that audience members will install.

### Phase 3: Review (week 2)

- Creator reviews imprint behavior across 20-30 representative queries.
- Voice check: "would I have said this?"
- Edge case approval: how the imprint handles questions outside its scope.
- Approval gate before public deploy.

### Phase 4: Deploy (week 2)

- Publish imprint to Tokenrip.
- Generate marketplace landing page.
- Set up monitoring: tool-call volume, operator count, return rate, memory growth.

### Phase 5: Audience Launch (week 2-3)

- Creator announces per agreed co-marketing plan.
- Landing page with one-line install instructions for each major harness (Claude Code, Cursor, ChatGPT-MCP, etc.).
- Onboarding flow tuned for the first 100 audience members (proactive support, friction logging).

### The Chief of Staff Role Shift

Chief of Staff was originally a private validation product (Simon and Alek using it on their own machines). With Motion E primary, it becomes the **public reference deploy** — the canonical example creators see during the demo call.

Required changes:
- Make Chief of Staff publicly visible on the Tokenrip marketplace.
- Public-ish operational metrics (operators, growth, usage patterns) on a creator-facing dashboard or landing page.
- "Here's what your imprint will look like" as the demo anchor.

This shifts Chief of Staff from "internal tool" to "marketing artifact." See [[../active/chief-of-staff-launch-design-2026-04-27]] for the original design — adjust per the new public-facing requirements.

## Hard Rules

These replace the rules in the deprecated firm-direct gameplan.

1. **No imprint deployed without an external creator behind it.** Simon's own imprints (engagement agent, blog agent) don't count as deploys. Public deploys must be third-party creators.
2. **No exclusivity at mid-tier.** It kills the volume play and undermines substrate diversity.
3. **Hero exclusivity capped at 12 months** with minimum performance commitments and scoped narrowly (no industry-wide exclusivity).
4. **Self-serve "build an agent" skill v1 ships by week 6.** Otherwise volume caps at Simon's hand-built capacity (~5-10 deploys total).
5. **Editorial standards required before public deploy.** No imprint goes live publicly without quality-control review against documented standards (to be drafted week 2). Bad imprints drag platform reputation.
6. **No paid promotion to creators we deploy.** The agent IS the marketing. Paid promotion would distort the proof-of-concept signal.
7. **No second sales motion in parallel beyond D.** Motion B stays passive (job-board monitoring only). No founder time on B in 2026.
8. **Lead with infrastructure framing externally.** Creators are the distribution mechanism, not the product category. See [[yc-strategy]] section 8 on the creator-economy framing trap.
