## How Stauss built his network (the "spider web" → the market-intelligence vision)

**Stauss:** This is how I built my network. I started with anyone who talked to me about any debt deal — "send it over." Most had nothing for me, but then I'd go to LinkedIn, find an inventory or ABL direct lender, message a hundred of them: "I've got a potential deal, let me know if you want to connect." Out of 150, ~20 talked, 2 were a fit. I'm not regulated, so I either take a finder's/broker fee, or we just keep in contact and pass deal flow back and forth. Doing that, I made connections, started DMing, grew from 2,000 to 5,000–8,000 followers. Connection requests: "I work with lenders and capital providers doing $1–50M debt deals for companies doing $10M to a billion+ in revenue." It became a spider web that brought me a ton of deals. Took three and a half years.

So the market intelligence should be that spider web. If I get a deal that's not a fit because there's no equipment, I plug it in and it says: "you have seven ABL lenders here; you've closed with two; these two didn't respond" — and ranks them. Rank by credit fit AND by responsiveness, professionalism, trustworthiness — the human stuff you get on a call. I want to keep feeding deal flow to the guys who actually close and pay me. Two days ago a guy told me "this is great except they're only doing $1M revenue, we require $5M+." If I'd had that in here it would have X'd him out — I wouldn't have wasted time.

## Bevel ≠ broker; the fraud-detection theme

**Aleksandar:** Bevel seems to fit — friction in two spaces: underwriting deals instantly, and placing deals across fragmented data.

**Stauss:** Clarify: Bevel is a specialty financial-services firm, not a broker. Their service is significant tax benefits (bonus depreciation) to companies and HNW/UHNW individuals. They buy equipment, put it in EquipmentShare and share revenue — but the real money is the tax benefits. So Bevel is niche specialty, not a traditional broker.

**Aleksandar:** Operationally, any difference between Bevel and a broker beyond requirements?

**Stauss:** Many "direct lenders/underwriters" aren't. I could white-label VFI's criteria, call it Stauss Paulos LLC, claim I underwrite and fund — but the bank/capital provider does. Small-ticket underwriting is ratio-driven, algorithmic: been around six months? Bank statements? Tax returns? If-this-then-this. For VFI it'd be very challenging to fully automate underwriting — we can expedite it. We find crazy stuff: a guy refinancing an asset to hide income in his divorce; owners in lawsuits, criminal activity. My credit team does significant research on ownership.

**Fraud — this is crucial, ties to UCC.** We had the best-looking trucking company — audits, growing revenue. Came back for a second deal and something felt off. Turned them down. Months later, *60 Minutes* on Russian/Serbian mafia money laundering in Chicago trucking — that was the kind of profile. There's a strong fraud-detection component. Another: a broker brought a band-aid deal, equipment + real estate collateral, we funded $2M, they missed the FIRST payment — turned out the broker got $80K, the title/escrow agent $50K, another guy $40K, ~$170–300K disbursed right away, then slow-pay. Unethical/pseudo-fraudulent.

**The big one — UCC + double-financing.** A client hid his UCC filings using a vague filing (vs. a specific filing listing the actual equipment). He'd already financed his equipment, got $2–3M, came to us, the filing didn't match up, we funded — he double-financed. Owed loan sharks in Vegas. And **First Brands** — multi-billion tier-one auto manufacturer, financed over a billion, double/triple-financing equipment and inventory across lenders. One lender heavily involved was our competitor **Onset Financial** (Utah). Onset created a fund, took local investor money AND money from First Brands VPs/executives, to fund the fraudulent deals. Now a billion-dollar lawsuit. Fraud is way more prevalent than you'd think — documents that look legit aren't. That's why we're firm on auditor reviews; I could fake better than internally-prepared QuickBooks financials all day.

[Stauss also detailed, during Deal B: auditor-legitimacy verification — a doctored audit had only a signature, no logo/watermark/location, "doesn't read like how DBP typically issues their audit" → fraud-alert flag. And his read process: fiscal vs. calendar year-end determination; current assets/AR/prepaids; PP&E ("I like 10–20M"); due-from-related-parties note; finance vs. operating leases; current portion of long-term debt; deduct goodwill/intangibles from equity → tangible net worth → require/waive personal guarantee; revenue/gross-profit/net-income trend; add back D&A for cash-flow; rate negotiation by reverse-engineering the debt note for other lenders' pricing.]
