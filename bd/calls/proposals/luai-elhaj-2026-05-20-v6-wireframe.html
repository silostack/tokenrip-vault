Pilot proposal: Post-engagement case review console for Get In Canada

A client submits a questionnaire, the Get In Canada team reviews it, the file goes to IRCC, and it comes back: "you didn't sign this, your birth certificate isn't stamped." Another envelope. Another week. The console proposed below runs that review in one pass — discrepancies flagged, follow-up emails drafted, the reviewer in the loop on every case before anything goes out.

How the workflow runs

  1. Intake Questionnaire and documents land in the case console. One screen per case, all source material in one place.
  2. AI pass Every field cross-referenced against every document. Discrepancies flagged with source, conflict, and confidence score.
  3. Drafts prepared Missing-information follow-up emails to the client written and queued for review. No back-and-forth — one consolidated message per case.
  4. Reviewer console The case manager sees the full picture in one screen: questionnaire, documents, flags, draft email, AI reasoning. Designed for fast review, not investigation.
  5. Approve, reject, annotate The reviewer approves the draft, rejects with a note, or adds a flag the AI missed. Nothing is sent without this step.
  6. The system learns Reviewer notes feed back into the model. The next case is sharper. The team's expertise compounds inside the system rather than getting bypassed by it.

Types of issues the system catches

The list below is representative, not exhaustive. The system is calibrated to Get In Canada's specific client population and document mix during the pilot.

  • Name spelling conflicts (questionnaire vs. passport)
  • Date-of-birth format mismatches (DD/MM vs. MM/DD)
  • Passport expiry vs. travel dates
  • Missing signatures and stamps
  • Document and form field conflicts
  • Blank answers vs. "N/A" (military service, prior refusals)
  • Employment and education date gaps
  • Travel history gaps
  • Illegible scans flagged before submission
  • Custom checks built to the Get In Canada workflow
The questionnaire varies by application type — study permit, visitor visa, work permit, Express Entry, family sponsorship, citizenship — but the workflow above is the same across all of them. Phase 2: B2B back-office expansion

Get In Canada is positioned to operate a back-office model for other immigration consultants — those firms bring clients, the Get In Canada team handles file preparation, and the firm charges a fraction of what an in-house case manager would cost.

The structural constraint on that model today is throughput per reviewer. Manual case review caps how many files each person can process, and quality risk scales with volume. The Phase 1 console removes both constraints inside one firm.

Phase 2 is the same console operating in multi-tenant mode — each external consultant's cases routed through Get In Canada's reviewers via the same interface, with tenant-level permissions and routing. No new product to build; the Phase 1 console is the Phase 2 platform, just with more tenants flowing through it. Each reviewer handles a multiple of today's volume at the same headcount, and the back-office becomes a scalable business rather than a labor-margin play.

Pricing for Phase 2 is modeled on file volume rather than per-seat SaaS, and is scoped together once Phase 1 is operating and the unit economics are visible.
