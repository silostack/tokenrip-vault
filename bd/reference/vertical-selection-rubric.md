# Vertical Selection — Weighted Rubric

**Purpose**: Pick the first vertical for the design partner pilot. Optimize for **speed to test**: how fast can we get from pitch → call → demo → signed deal?

**Status**: **Decision locked 2026-05-09.** Three verticals selected. See decision section at bottom.

**Original status**: Yoda's pressure-test of the most likely candidates. Alek has additional candidates from his research — score those into the same rubric.

## The Criteria

Seven criteria, weighted to total 100. Ordered by weight (heaviest first).

| # | Criterion | Weight | Why this weight |
|---|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Sales cycle length** | **30** | Simon's stated priority. We're testing demand fast. A vertical where decisions take 3 months kills the test. Brokerages and small-firm partnerships beat enterprise SaaS sales here. |
| 2 | **Warm lead density / unfair access** | **20** | First deals come from warm intros. Cold-to-pilot in 6 weeks is unrealistic. Where we have unfair access (existing relationships, founder domain knowledge), we have a real shot. |
| 3 | **Demo legibility** | **15** | The demo has to be self-explanatory in 3 minutes. Universally-understood workflows (real estate closings, claims) beat insider-jargon workflows (tax practice management). |
| 4 | **Domain knowledge density** | **15** | Building a vertical pilot blind eats time. We need enough knowledge to ask sharp discovery questions and not get caught flat-footed on basics. |
| 5 | **Procurement complexity** | **10** | Heavy regulation (insurance, healthcare) = security review, vendor onboarding, legal review. Light procurement (small firms, brokerages) = owner says yes, deal moves. |
| 6 | **Adjacency reuse** | **5** | Nice to have. Tax → bookkeeping → audit is easier copy-over than tax → real estate. But Simon explicitly weighted this low; speed-first. |
| 7 | **Decision-maker accessibility** | **5** | Some industries gatekeep buyers (enterprise CTOs, compliance officers). Others put the buyer one LinkedIn message away (broker-owners, managing partners). |

**Scoring**: Each vertical scored 1–10 on each criterion. Multiply by weight. Sum to 1000-point total.

## Per-Vertical Reasoning

### Real Estate (residential brokerage / brokerage tech) — **815 / 1000**

**Why it scores high**:
- **Sales cycle (8)**: Broker-owners decide directly. Independent brokerages can sign in 2-3 weeks. Even franchise/network operators decide faster than vertical SaaS.
- **Warm leads (8)**: Simon has CTO friend at RE tech co. Domain network through prior life. Plus universally understood vocabulary — even cold prospects "get" the pitch fast.
- **Demo legibility (10)**: Closing process is the textbook example of "many parties, many documents, easy to fall apart." Show a demo of "buyer submits docs → agent reviews → routes to title → closes" and any human alive understands it.
- **Domain knowledge (8)**: Simon has worked in/around RE. Knows the participants, the documents, the failure modes.
- **Procurement (8)**: Brokerages are small businesses. Owner says yes, deal moves. No security review or vendor onboarding theater.

**Where it loses points**:
- Adjacency to other verticals is moderate — RE → mortgage → title is a chain, but extending to insurance/legal requires more rebuild.

**Specific targets to research**:
- Mid-size residential brokerages (10-50 agents) with tech-forward leadership
- Boutique commercial brokerages
- RE tech companies (proptech) that serve brokerages — possible co-sell motion
- Title companies and closing coordinators (adjacent persona)

### Bookkeeping / Outsourced Accounting — **630 / 1000**

**Why it scores well**:
- **Sales cycle (7)**: Founder-led bookkeeping firms decide fast.
- **Demo legibility (8)**: Month-end close is well-understood, document-heavy, multi-stakeholder (firm + multiple clients).
- **Procurement (8)**: Small firms.

**Where it loses points**:
- **Warm leads (4)**: We don't have a specific contact in this space.
- **Domain knowledge (5)**: General finance literacy but not bookkeeping-specific operations.

**Why it's a dark horse**: high velocity, simple decision-making, demo is intuitive. If real estate stalls, this is the obvious second choice — and bookkeeping → fractional CFO → tax has good adjacency for vertical #2.

### Consulting (boutique strategy / domain firms) — **615 / 1000**

**Why it's interesting**:
- Managing partners decide fast in small partnerships
- Simon has consulting orientation and likely some network

**Why it's risky**:
- Every consulting firm's workflow is different — deliverable types, review cycles, client mgmt all vary. The demo doesn't generalize as well within "consulting" as it does within "real estate."
- Adjacency is poor — winning here doesn't help with vertical #2.

**Verdict**: Avoid as primary unless Alek has a specific warm lead.

### Tax / Accounting — **575 / 1000**

**Why it doesn't lead**:
- **Sales cycle (5)**: Cyclical business. Q1 = busy season, no one buys. Q2-Q4 slower but still seasonal.
- **TaxDome already explored** and the partner-buyer is fear-driven, not innovation-driven.
- **Warm leads (6)**: Petar is warm but indirect — he's at the platform layer (TaxDome), not at a tax firm.
- Domain vocabulary creates demo friction.

**Where it shines**: Adjacency reuse is strongest — tax → bookkeeping → audit → fractional CFO → wealth advisory is a connected market. If we go tax first, the next 3 verticals become much easier. But that's the long game; we're optimizing for speed-first.

**Verdict**: Strong second-or-third pick if real estate doesn't move. Reconsider when mounted agent demo is ready (could re-open TaxDome).

### M&A Boutique Investment Banking — **520 / 1000**

**Why it's middle of the pack**:
- Deal package routing is a real pain (data rooms, redlines, closing checklists)
- Partners are busy and hard to schedule
- No warm lead, limited domain knowledge
- Procurement reasonable but partners hard to commit

**Verdict**: Skip. Long sales cycles, hard access, no edge.

### Construction — **515 / 1000**

**Why it doesn't lead**:
- No warm lead
- Limited domain knowledge
- RFI/submittal workflow makes intuitive sense (good demo legibility) but everything else is cold

**Verdict**: Skip unless Alek's research turned up something specific.

### Insurance — **370 / 1000** (rubric score) → **Selected despite rubric**

**Why the rubric scored it low**:
- Heavily regulated → long procurement, security review, compliance
- Enterprise-style sales cycles even for "small" insurers
- No warm lead
- Limited domain knowledge

**Why it was selected anyway (2026-05-09)**:
Subsequent vertical research revealed a critical factor the rubric missed: **supply-side COI automation has zero competitors.** Every COI player (myCOI, PINS, Certus) tracks COIs that companies *receive*. Nobody helps agencies *process and issue* them. This zero-competition angle changes the calculus — you're not entering a competitive market, you're creating one.

Additional research findings:
- 40,000+ independent P&C agencies in the US; sweet spot = 15,000-20,000 with $1M-$20M revenue
- CSRs spend 60-80% of time on repetitive operational tasks
- SAM: $270M-$720M at $1,500-3,000/month pricing
- ROI: one CSR costs $45-65K loaded; automating 50% of COI processing pays for the product immediately

The rubric correctly identified procurement complexity and access challenges. The bet is that the zero-competition angle + clear ROI justification overcome those barriers for independent agencies (which have simpler procurement than carriers).

**The angel investor named insurance as a target.** Yoda's original read: park it for vertical #2 or #3. Post-research read: the COI angle is strong enough to justify a parallel test with Simon owning it directly.
