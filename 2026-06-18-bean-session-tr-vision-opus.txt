  stored history; it's that blame/log/grep make history addressable. Operational
  work, even when stored, had no recall mechanism. A shared drive of 5k files
  is an archive, not a memory.

  the why-graph from a graveyard into a flywheel: every new decision surfaces
  the most-relevant past decisions and their outcomes at the moment of choosing.
  Without it, the why-graph is write-only ‚ÄĒ you can record reasoning but never
  get it back at the point it matters. With it, the substrate actually feeds the
  next decision. That's the loop the deck claims but hasn't named the mechanism
  for.

  ‚Ėé Memory you can't recall is an archive. Git gave code associative recall. 
  ‚Ėé Tokenrip + semantic search gives every other kind of work the same ‚ÄĒ not 
  ‚Ėé just a record of what happened and why, but the relevant past surfaced at 
  ‚Ėé the exact moment of the next decision. That's what turns a shared drive into
  ‚Ėé a shared mind.

  The trap: memory is invisible and slow ‚ÄĒ the enemy of a 60-second demo
