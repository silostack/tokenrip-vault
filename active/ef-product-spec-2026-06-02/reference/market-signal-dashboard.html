Maritime & Jones Act Fleet

SHIPS Act tailwind · aged fleet · specialty residuals · underserved by banks
— On Water · Underserved Vertical

A 651K-job industry where the average OSV is over 14 years old — and the SHIPS Act of 2025 just lit a match.

The U.S. domestic maritime industry runs on a 41,000-vessel Jones Act fleet — roughly 5,500 tugs, 31,500 barges, plus tankers, OSVs, dredges, ferries, and fishing vessels. The law requires every vessel moving cargo between U.S. ports to be U.S.-built, U.S.-owned, U.S.-flagged, and U.S.-crewed.

That captive-market structure has aged badly. The active Jones Act OSV fleet averages over 14 years old. The government-owned sealift fleet averages 44 years old. Replacement isn't optional — Subchapter M compliance, Coast Guard inspection failures, and rising maintenance costs are forcing operator decisions every quarter.

The SHIPS Act of 2025 (Shipbuilding and Harbor Infrastructure for Prosperity and Security for America Act) is the regulatory forcing function. Bipartisan support, Trump administration backing, private equity entering aggressively. It's the maritime version of EPA 2027 for trucking — a known, dated, capital-mobilizing event.

The financing wall is the same story you read in tier-2 AI infra: banks finance the hulls they understand (oceangoing tankers, big-name containerships) but back off on the long tail — workboats, push boats, OSVs, dredging equipment, marine cranes, propulsion repowers. Limited shipyard capacity (Edison Chouest built 63% of the DP2-class PSV fleet) means pricing power sits with a few yards, and build times of 3+ years require capital that moves before the yard slot closes.

This is exactly Northbeam's lane. Residual-based leasing on specialty marine assets, full project financing including soft costs, 2-4 week deal turnaround. Most equipment lenders don't touch it. Wells Fargo and a small handful play in the larger tickets — Northbeam's $3-30M sweet spot at the workboat / push-boat / OSV refit / dredge level is wide open.

The OBBBA Tax Shift

100% bonus depreciation now permanent · changes the pitch
— §168(k) · §168(n) · §179

The year-end tax urgency pitch is dead. The equipment urgency pitch is louder.

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 4, 2025) permanently restored 100% bonus depreciation for property placed in service after January 19, 2025 — reversing the TCJA phasedown that would have hit 20% in 2026 and zero in 2027.

What this means for Northbeam's sales motion: do not lead with year-end tax urgency. Customers who wait six months get the same 100% deduction. The urgency now is structural — delivery lead times, EPA 2027 deadlines, capacity locks, and rising used values. That's a stronger story, not a weaker one.

The new Section 168(n) Qualified Production Property is the sleeper for foreign-OEM and reshoring deals: manufacturers can immediately expense the production-floor portion of new facility costs that previously depreciated over 39 years.

— Desk Brief · 12 May 2026

The replacement cycle arrived. The tax timer vanished. Now sell on delivery, not deduction.

Three things changed in the last 60 days that should re-anchor every BDO's pitch deck. First, OBBBA made 100% bonus depreciation permanent — the December tax-rush close is gone. Second, the average Class 8 tractor in the US is now 6.3 years old, the highest in over a decade, and 69% of vocational fleets report being past their normal replacement cycle. Third, used Class 8 retail prices ticked up to $56,128 in March — the first sustained sign that the secondary market is firming.

The macro signals on this dashboard — PMI 52.7, core capex +9.4% YoY, ELFA CFI at a record quarter — describe the destination. The vintage and used-equipment data describe the mechanism. The 2018–2020 buy bulge is hitting its 6-year mark exactly when EPA 2027 raises the cost of waiting and when AI-related capex is locking up OEM build slots. That's not a forecast; that's a pipeline.

What changes operationally: BDOs should retire any pitch that leads with "close before year-end for the tax benefit." It's empirically wrong now and customers' CFOs will know it. The new lead should be lead time — switchgear at 50–80 weeks, BESS at 36–60, Class 8 tractors at 28–36 with H2 2026 OEM slots already 60% gone, CNC at 20–32. Whoever finances first locks the slot.

The risk being tracked: Iran war knocked ELFA confidence 13 points off February's peak even while real volume sits at record. If volume converges down to sentiment in Q3 rather than sentiment converging up to volume, the regulatory-forced cohort (EPA fleets, AI infra) holds up while discretionary expansion capex cools. Northbeam is structurally protected here — our deal box is already weighted toward regulatory and replacement-driven deals. Lean into that, not against it.
