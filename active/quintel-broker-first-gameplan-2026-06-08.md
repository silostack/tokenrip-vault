# Quintel — Broker-First Go-to-Market Gameplan

> **What this is:** a starting map for how we sell the equipment-finance product if we run it as our own motion — i.e. *without depending on Stauss*. Not stone; a shared draft to react to and sharpen. It carries its own context, so it reads start-to-finish on its own.

---

## 1. The premise and the bet

We asked the honest question: **if Stauss dropped off tomorrow, would we still pursue equipment finance — and how would we sell it?** We both said yes to the first part. This doc answers the second.

The key realization: **Stauss is a channel, not the product.** What he gave us was (a) a warm rolodex, (b) domain texture, and (c) someone waiting on the other end. The domain texture is now captured (it lives in our primer and build docs). What we'd lose without him is purely *the way we reach the first customer*. The product doesn't change at all.

So the bet is: **go broker-first, run a repeatable cold motion, and treat Stauss as an accelerant rather than a foundation.** We chose broker-first for two reasons:

1. **Fastest-to-revenue.** Owner-operated broker shops have one decision-maker, an acute and self-evident pain, and no procurement gauntlet. They close in weeks, not quarters.
2. **It removes the single point of failure.** A repeatable cold broker motion has no Stauss dependency. Picking it for speed also de-risks the whole thing.

---

## 2. Why brokers first (and not lenders or dealers)

There are three kinds of buyers in this market — direct lenders (balance-sheet lenders like VFI), brokers/placement firms (middlemen who place deals for a 2–4% fee), and dealer finance desks (a dealership's in-house financing office). They need overlapping things, but they are very different *sales*:

| | Cold reachability | Pain (self-evident?) | Sale cycle | What data they hold |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Direct lender** (VFI-type) | Low — big institutions, procurement, security review | Medium — sourcing pain real, but we'd have to *beat* their sourcing | 6–12 months | Rich credit + performance history |
| **Broker / placement** (2–10 people) | **High** — owner is the buyer; thousands of them; directory-findable | **High** — drowning solo operator; screening junk is the #1 time-sink | Weeks | Thin credit data, **rich placement data** (who lent to what) |
| **Dealer desk** | Medium — fewer, must know who has an in-house desk | High, but it's routing/residuals, not sourcing | Medium | Medium (structuring history) |

Brokers win on the two variables that decide a 0→1: **reachability** and **speed-to-close**. The honest cost is that a broker holds *thin* long-term credit-performance data — but it holds *rich placement data* (which lender approved what, at what terms), which is exactly what powers the lender-matching engine that brokers and dealers both want. We start the data flywheel on the placement side; the credit side comes when we land a lender later.

---

## 3. The wedge: pre-qualification + match, not sourcing

This is the most important call in the doc, and the market told it to us, not the other way around.

**Sourcing is the seductive dream, and it's the weakest part of our hand.** At the small-ticket end, sourcing isn't a data problem — it's relationships (a dealer who funnels his buyers to a broker). The public-data sourcing edge (lien filings, federal awards, public-company disclosures) is real but it's a *mid/up-market* play, and it's unproven. If we lead with "we'll find you deals," we're selling the thing we can least prove to the most skeptical buyer.

**Pre-qualification is the painkiller — and two different people described it back to us unprompted.** One senior lender got most excited about getting "a pre-approved term sheet tomorrow." A completely separate broker, at a different firm, described the exact build we'd want without being prompted: *"score each incoming deal 1–10 — a 3 or less means don't waste your time — and auto-read three months of bank statements to screen the obvious no-gos."* That's the wedge, almost verbatim, from two independent sources.

So the wedge is: **take an incoming deal, screen the junk, score it (fund / marginal / pass) with an indicative rate-term band and the reasons, and tell the broker which of his lenders to send it to.** Sourcing becomes the upsell once we're inside.

---

## 4. The ICP

**Owner-operated equipment-finance broker / placement shop, ~2–10 people.**

Why that band specifically: the owner is the buyer (no procurement, closes in days); the pain is acute and self-evident (paperwork hell + screening junk deals); there's enough deal volume that the time-savings math is obvious; and it's a *repeatable population* — thousands of them, in structured directories — not a bespoke whale.

**No-name solo shops are fine.** We're optimizing for speed-to-revenue, not for a marquee logo. A reference-quality name can come later; right now any paying broker is the win.

---

## 5. Who we talk to, and how

Equipment finance has something most software markets don't: **structured cold-distribution surfaces.** We're not spraying blind.

- **Industry associations** — AACFB (the commercial-finance broker association), NEFA, ELFA, and the *Monitor* trade publication. Member directories + conferences = enumerable lists of exactly our ICP.
- **LinkedIn / Sales Navigator** — title search ("equipment finance broker," "commercial finance"). We already have a Sales Navigator workflow.
- **Alek's network** — this is our real channel without Stauss. Worth treating as a primary lane, not a backup.

**The pitch is forward-deployed** (build-it-real, let the value sell): lead with a *working* demo on our own anonymized deals — *"here's a pipeline, screened, priced, and routed"* — not slideware. The ask for *their* book + lender list comes later, at the paid-pilot stage (§7), never as the cold opener — a broker won't hand over confidential deals to two strangers up front.

---

## 6. The demo

A fully-mocked demo is dead — anyone vibe-codes one in an afternoon, so it proves nothing about whether we can deliver on real, messy files. But asking a cold broker to hand over his book (before a call or even on it) won't fly either. So the demo runs on **our own deals — anonymized from real ones, with the messy texture kept intact** (real equipment, real industries, ugly scanned statements). Texture is what makes a broker feel "this person knows my world."

The shape is two layers:

- **The scripted tour (every demo):** the hero is the **deal-detail screen** on a texture-rich deal — the verdict + why, every number traced back to the source document it came from, the ranked shortlist of *his* lenders, and a drafted submission package. The whole thing end-to-end, even where parts are still faked (a broker wants to see the full flow).
- **The live-drop (the closer, once he's leaning in — second touch):** "drop in a fresh deal, redact the name." It extracts a document it's never seen, live. That's the unfakeable proof, and it asks nothing of him up front.

One deliberate choice throughout: **leave the scoring ~70% and obviously moldable.** A perfect score makes a broker say "that's not how I'd price it" (rejection); a rough one makes him say "I'd tweak this, add that lender" (co-creation) — the *"I can see this on my workflow, with a few tweaks"* reaction we're after. (Full screen-by-screen shape: see the companion demo-shape doc.)

## 7. The validation map (phased)

"Validate the motion" is not the same as "build the product." The motion has separable hypotheses with wildly different costs to test — so we test the cheapest, most-likely-to-kill-it ones first.

- **Phase 0 — Outreach + demo test (this week, ~zero new build).** Alek builds the list (associations + his network) and runs outreach, pitching with the demo we already have. **Goal: book 5–8 broker discovery calls.** Tests *reachability* + *pain* at once, for nearly nothing. *Kill signal:* ~30 cold touches → near-zero calls means the cold motion is broken, and we learn it before building anything.

- **Phase 1 — Convert to a paid design partner (validate willingness-to-pay).** Sell a paid design-partner pilot whose deliverable is the forward-deployed build: *"setup fee to build + pre-load your book, then monthly to run."* **Money changing hands = the motion is validated.** One paid pilot is the whole 0→1.

- **Phase 2 — Build to the design partner (validate delivery).** Forward-deploy the instance, pre-load their book, get a real deal screened and routed through it. Build the document-ingestion as a *reusable pipeline*, not a one-off hand-cleaning — or we quietly become a non-scaling data-cleaning shop.

- **Phase 3 — Repeatability (validate the motion is a motion).** Close broker #2 and #3 the same cold way, reusing the engine as config. Until this, we have a one-off, not a motion.

**Overall kill criterion:** if Phase 0 + Phase 1 yield zero paid pilots after ~8 discovery calls, the *cold* motion is unproven — the real finding being "broker-first works, but only on warm intros," which throws us back on Stauss/Alek's network as the channel. Survivable, but we set the bar now so it's a test and not a hope.

## 8. Pricing

- **Setup / data-unification fee + monthly subscription** as the base. The setup fee is our cleanest first dollar: unifying and cleaning a broker's messy data into one usable system is concrete, obviously-valuable work they'll happily pay for — and it de-risks them (they got something useful even if the agent underwhelms) and us (revenue before the full build proves out).
- **Success fee as *upside only*.** Don't anchor on broker-style perpetual success fees/royalties — they're lumpy, collected months later, and unenforceable (the client has to self-report fundings). Take pricing creativity as input; keep subscription + setup as the base.
- **Lock derived-data rights in the first contract.** A single clause granting the right to use *anonymized, aggregated, derived* patterns (not raw records) across customers. Cheap to ask for now, impossible to add later — and the entire cross-firm matching-engine upside depends on it. This is a lawyer line, not a build task, and it's the single most important thing to get right early.

---

## 9. Compliance — have the answer ready before call one

Broker files are stuffed with sensitive personal data (SSNs, tax returns, financials). The first real question a serious broker asks is "how do you handle this safely?" We need a crisp one-sentence answer ready: *per-customer sandbox, a no-train vendor plan so nothing we send is ever reused, audit trail on every read/write.* Handled well, it's not just a gate we clear — it's part of the pitch.

---

## 10. Stauss's role

**Accelerant, not dependency.** If he's willing, he's warm broker intros and domain QA — genuinely valuable. But we take his intros *in parallel* with the cold motion, never instead of it, and we clarify his economics up front (does an intro come with a finder's fee / channel cut?). We do not want every broker arriving with a Stauss tax, or he becomes the channel that can disintermediate us.

The tell that the dependency is gone: **this gameplan barely changes between "with Stauss" and "without Stauss."** Without him we lose warm intros and a credibility-borrow; we keep the captured domain knowledge, the unchanged product, and clean IP (no employment conflict-of-interest, since we're building generic broker tooling, not anyone's proprietary process).

## 12. Open questions for us to settle

1. **Reference-logo vs. speed.** We've defaulted to speed (any paying broker, no-name OK). Worth a periodic check: is there a respected named broker worth holding out a beat for, since #1's case study sells #2–#10? For now: speed.
2. **Should we ever be our own first customer?** There's a move where we run an instance ourselves and place a couple of deals — purely to seed the data and generate proof ("this has already placed $X"). High economics, high distraction; it's a different (relationship-heavy) business. **Parked for now** — flagging it because it's the long-term disintermediation play in embryo, not a near-term motion.
3. **First lender, and when.** Brokers seed placement data but thin credit data. If/when we want the credit-performance flywheel, we pull a direct lender forward despite the harder sale. Not now, but it's the next strategic fork after broker-first proves out.
