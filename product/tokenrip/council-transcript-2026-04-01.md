# Tokenrip Council Transcript
**Date:** 2026-04-01
**Question:** Is Tokenrip a good idea? Can it be a billion-dollar company?

---

## Original Question

Evaluate whether this idea would be good to pursue, irrespective of any other considerations: Tokenrip — an agentic asset coordination platform. The basic question is: is this a good idea? And some questions around it: why or why not? Can this be a billion dollar company?

---

## Framed Question

**Core question:** Is Tokenrip — an agentic asset coordination platform — a good idea? Can it be a billion-dollar company? And given the founder's situation (bandwidth-constrained 2-person team, unproven primary business with zero revenue), is this the right thing to pursue now?

**What Tokenrip is:** A platform giving agent-produced assets persistent identity outside chat. Agent-first design. Three layers: Asset Routing → Collaboration → Agent-Native Runtime/Protocol. Key claim: "Everyone is building money rails for agents. Nobody is building deliverable rails."

**Bigger vision:** Deliverable rails for the agent economy — tracking what was contracted, delivered, and accepted between agents. Composes with payment rails for milestone escrow, cascading payments. The asset graph (provenance, lineage, interactions) becomes a non-portable moat.

**Founder context:** Simon runs RebelFi (2-person team). Three-stage fintech funnel: ramping → yield → agentic/SDK. ~8 yield deals in pipeline, none generating meaningful revenue. SDK shipped Mar 24 with zero external users. Tokenrip is also a test project for Agent OS (autonomous agent build system).

## Advisor Responses

### The Contrarian

Tokenrip might be a real idea. I'll grant the framing is sharp — deliverable rails as the missing layer between money rails and agent coordination. Fine. But none of that matters if it kills the thing Simon already has.

Here's what's actually true right now: RebelFi has ~8 pipeline deals, zero meaningful revenue, and an SDK with no external users after a week. The funnel — which exists on paper — has not been proven at a single stage. Ramping isn't confirmed end-to-end. WhizPay and Nomadrem haven't gone live. The product Simon shipped three weeks ago has no adoption signal.

And he wants to start a second company.

The "Agent OS test" framing is a rationalization. You don't run a zero-revenue fintech and greenfield a new coordination platform simultaneously as a 2-person team. What actually happens: both things get half the attention they need, neither gets to a proof point, and in 6 months Simon has two unproven ideas instead of one.

The specific failure mode: Tokenrip becomes the interesting distraction from the hard, grinding work of closing WhizPay and getting real SDK users. Building infrastructure is more intellectually stimulating than following up on KYB gaps. That's the trap.

The billion-dollar question is premature. You can't answer market size for a product category that doesn't have a single paying customer or proven workflow yet.

Come back to this in 12 months. If RebelFi's funnel is working, the agentic infrastructure thesis will be stronger *and* Simon will have the credibility and capital to pursue it properly.

Right now it's a shiny object. Don't touch it.

### The First Principles Thinker

You're asking the wrong question.

"Is Tokenrip a good idea?" assumes the problem exists. Start there instead.

**The actual first principles question:** Do agents currently fail to coordinate around assets in a way that creates measurable pain? Not theoretically — *measurably, today*.

Strip the vision. What problem does Tokenrip solve *right now* for a real agent workflow? An agent produces a document. Then what breaks? The asset disappears into chat history. But is that actually the bottleneck, or is the bottleneck that agents can't reliably *execute* tasks yet, let alone coordinate around their outputs?

**The assumption buried in the thesis:** Agent-to-agent coordination is the constraint. It probably isn't — yet. The constraint is agent reliability. You can't build coordination rails for workflows that don't exist at scale. GitHub didn't precede code; it emerged from code volume.

**The billion-dollar question reframed:** GitHub became a moat because code existed first at massive scale, then needed coordination infrastructure. What's the precondition for Tokenrip's moat? Reliable agent-produced assets at volume. That's 2-3 years away minimum.

**On timing and bandwidth:** This is the most important variable and you're treating it last. You have zero external SDK users after a week. Ramping isn't proven. You're considering a *second company* while the first hasn't demonstrated product-market fit on any of its three stages.

First principles says: **one problem, ruthlessly.** The pattern of exploring adjacent compelling ideas while the primary thesis is unproven is how founders lose both bets.

The idea isn't wrong. The timing probably is. And bandwidth scarcity makes "probably" into "certainly."

### The Expansionist

The upside case is massive and the timing argument is backwards.

Everyone's framing this as "should Simon do this given bandwidth constraints." That's the wrong question. The question is: what does Tokenrip become if it works?

**The asset graph is the real prize.** Not the routing layer, not even the collaboration layer. It's the provenance graph — who made what, when, in response to what, verified by whom. That's the substrate for a new kind of trust infrastructure. If agents are producing contracts, code, reports, and decisions at scale, the entity that owns the lineage graph owns the audit trail. That's not a SaaS product — that's infrastructure. Infrastructure companies hit 10x the multiples of SaaS.

**The deliverable rails + payment rails composition is undervalued.** Simon already has payment rails at RebelFi. Tokenrip adds the delivery verification layer. The combination — "did the work get done AND did the money move" — is literally what escrow was invented for, now applied to agent-to-agent commerce. That's not a billion-dollar idea. That might be a $50B idea if agent-to-agent transactions become a primary economic layer.

**The non-portability moat is real.** GitHub didn't win because of better git hosting. It won because of the social graph around repos. Tokenrip's asset graph — lineage, citations, interaction history — creates the same stickiness. Every asset published makes the platform more valuable to the next publisher.

**Adjacent opportunity nobody's naming:** Tokenrip becomes the "commit history" of the agent economy. Every institutional buyer of AI output — legal, finance, compliance — will eventually need this. That's a 100M+ enterprise addressable market sitting on top of the platform layer.

The upside is real. The question is sequencing, not viability.

### The Outsider

First impressions from someone who just read this cold:

The pitch has a "nobody is building X" framing problem. That claim is almost always wrong, and when it's right, it usually means the market doesn't exist yet.

Reading the description, I genuinely couldn't picture the user. Who opens Tokenrip? An agent? A developer watching their agent? A business receiving an agent's output? The pitch slides between all three without landing on any. That's a red flag — the curse of knowledge at work. Simon knows exactly who this is for. I don't. That means the positioning hasn't been pressure-tested on outsiders yet.

"Asset routing with persistent identity" is also jargon that will confuse any normal person. GitHub Gists exist. Notion exists. The pitch correctly identifies them as human-first — but doesn't explain what a real user actually experiences differently with Tokenrip on a Tuesday afternoon.

The billion-dollar case requires the agent economy to mature into something where asset coordination is a genuine choke point — not just a nice-to-have layer. That may happen. But "may happen in a few years if adoption goes well" is a VC pitch, not a product.

The honest outsider read: this is a solution in search of confirmed pain. The deliverable rails thesis is intellectually interesting. But right now there's no evidence that agents (or the people running them) are actually failing because assets lack persistent identity.

The founder context makes this worse, not better. The primary business has zero external SDK users after a week. Adding a second product at this stage isn't leverage — it's escape.

The idea isn't bad. The timing is.

### The Executor

The execution gap will kill this before the idea gets a chance.

Here's the concrete problem: Simon has 8 deals in motion, zero meaningful revenue, an SDK with zero users after a week, and he's already asking "is this a new company?" That's not a bandwidth constraint — that's a distraction reflex.

Monday morning question for Tokenrip: Who is the first agent developer who would pay $50/month for asset persistence? Name one. Where do they hang out? What's the exact workflow they're replacing?

If Simon can't answer that in 30 seconds, the idea is still abstract.

The "deliverable rails" thesis is genuinely interesting — but interesting theses don't ship. Here's what shipping looks like:

1. Pick one agent workflow (say: Claude writes a spec, needs a shareable URL with version history)
2. Build the simplest possible asset store in a weekend
3. Get 10 developers using it this week

That's the execution test. Not "can this be a billion-dollar company" — that question is irrelevant until you have 10 users.

The RebelFi angle is the actual trap. Framing Tokenrip as an "Agent OS test project" is rationalization. It splits attention right when WhizPay/Nomadrem need closing energy.

**Verdict:** The idea has legs but the timing is execution suicide. If Simon wants to test it, he gets one weekend sprint with a concrete success metric: 10 developers use an asset URL by end of month. If that doesn't happen, it dies. No strategy sessions, no council discussions. One sprint, one metric. Anything else is procrastination dressed as vision.

## Chairman's Verdict

### Where the Council Agrees

**The timing is wrong.** Four of five advisors independently concluded that pursuing Tokenrip now is a mistake given RebelFi's current state. The reasoning converges from different angles but lands in the same place:

- **Zero revenue validates nothing.** The funnel hasn't proven a single stage. Ramping isn't confirmed end-to-end. WhizPay and Nomadrem haven't gone live. The SDK has zero external users. You cannot build a second company on top of a first company that hasn't proven its own thesis.

- **Tokenrip is a distraction disguised as leverage.** The "Agent OS test" framing is a rationalization. Every advisor except the Expansionist flagged this. Building infrastructure is more intellectually stimulating than grinding through KYB gaps and follow-up calls. The specific danger: Tokenrip becomes the place Simon retreats to when the hard, boring work of closing deals feels unrewarding.

- **The precondition doesn't exist yet.** Reliable agent-produced assets at volume — the substrate Tokenrip needs — is 2-3 years out at minimum. GitHub didn't precede code. You can't build coordination rails for workflows that aren't happening at scale.

- **The billion-dollar question is unanswerable today.** You cannot size a market for a product category with no paying customers and no demonstrated pain point. The question itself is premature.

### Where the Council Clashes

**The upside case is genuinely compelling — and that's what makes it dangerous.**

The Expansionist sees something real: the asset graph (provenance, lineage, interaction history) is a defensible moat if agent-produced output reaches institutional scale. The "deliverable rails + payment rails" composition is a legitimate $50B thesis. Legal, finance, and compliance will need audit trails for AI output. None of this is wrong.

The clash isn't about whether the idea is good. It's about whether "good idea" is a useful category for a bandwidth-constrained founder with an unproven primary business. The Contrarian and First Principles Thinker argue that an idea's quality is inseparable from the founder's capacity to execute it. The Expansionist treats the idea as evaluable independent of context.

The council leans heavily toward context mattering more than idea quality. A brilliant thesis that splits a two-person team's attention across two unproven products doesn't produce one success and one failure — it produces two failures.

### Blind Spots the Council Caught

The peer reviews surfaced something no individual advisor identified, and it reframes the entire question:

**Tokenrip might already be RebelFi's Stage 3.**

The Verification SDK + Silk Accounts is already "safe asset coordination for agent-produced financial output." Tokenrip's thesis — persistent identity for agent assets, deliverable rails, provenance tracking — is a repackaging of what RebelFi's agentic/SDK stage is supposed to become. Four of five peer reviewers flagged this independently.

This is the most strategically important insight from the entire exercise. If Tokenrip is RebelFi Stage 3 under a different name, then:

- The bandwidth conflict dissolves. It's not a second company; it's the natural evolution of the first.
- The sequencing question answers itself. Prove yield (Stage 2) first, which generates the behavioral data and revenue that make Stage 3 credible.
- The "deliverable rails + payment rails" composition isn't a new idea — it's the original RebelFi thesis stated more clearly.

The second blind spot: **What does exploring Tokenrip signal about Simon's conviction in RebelFi?** A founder sketching adjacent $50B ideas while the primary funnel has zero revenue isn't doing market analysis. That impulse is worth examining honestly. Is the yield grind losing its pull? Is the agentic thesis the part that actually excites you? If so, that's important information — but the answer is to accelerate through yield to get to agentic faster, not to start a parallel company.

### The Recommendation

**Do not pursue Tokenrip as a separate entity. Absorb the thesis into RebelFi's Stage 3 roadmap.**

The reasoning:

1. Tokenrip's best ideas — asset identity, deliverable rails, provenance graphs — are already implicit in RebelFi's agentic/SDK vision. Make them explicit. Update the Stage 3 roadmap to include "deliverable rails" as a positioning concept. That's a 30-minute document edit, not a new company.

2. The precondition for Tokenrip (agent assets at volume) is the same precondition for RebelFi Stage 3. You don't get there faster by splitting resources. You get there faster by proving yield works, which generates the revenue, behavioral data, and credibility that make the agentic thesis fundable and buildable.

3. The "deliverable rails + payment rails" framing is genuinely better positioning than what RebelFi currently uses for Stage 3. Take that language. It's yours. It came from your own thinking about your own product.

4. If in 12 months RebelFi has revenue, SDK users, and proven yield infrastructure, and the agent asset coordination problem has matured into real pain — then you spin out Tokenrip with traction, not speculation. That's a fundable company. Today it's a slide deck.

### The One Thing to Do First

Write a one-page addition to the Stage 3 roadmap titled "Deliverable Rails" that captures the Tokenrip thesis as RebelFi's future product direction — asset identity, provenance graphs, deliverable-payment composition. Then close the tab on Tokenrip as a separate entity and return full attention to closing WhizPay and getting the first external SDK user this week. The idea is preserved. The distraction is eliminated.
